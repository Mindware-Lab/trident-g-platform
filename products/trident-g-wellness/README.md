Catch burnout drift early and protect your best work. Zone Coach helps you spot when you’re in a good state for deep work, and when you need a short reset or a lighter block so you don’t waste hours pushing in the wrong state.

Translate routes into **work-appropriate next blocks**:

* **Deep work** → “90 min focus sprint + 10 min decompression”
* **Reset + retest** → “3–6 min downshift protocol, then re-check”

* **Recovery downshift** → “reduce demands, take a recovery block, earlier finish”

* If spun-out: suggest “walk-and-talk” or “camera off + notes” coping protocols.

* “State drift” trend over 24 days

## 1) Corporate wellness: fewer “bad-state work” hours, fewer burnout spirals

### A. It separates “productive intensity” from “burnout intensity”

In corporate settings, people commonly mistake *any* high arousal for “I’m on it”. Your split says:

* **Locked-in** = mobilised *and* regulated → best time for deep work, high-stakes decisions
* **Spun-out** = mobilised but dysregulated → high activity, lots of switching, more errors and conflict risk

That distinction reduces:

* overconfident decision-making on spun-out days
* reactive communication (email/Slack spirals)
* late-night “effort” that is actually state drift

### B. It gives a safe alternative to “push through”

Most corporate programmes offer generic advice (sleep, exercise, mindfulness). Your state model gives a **specific next block**:

* spun-out → reset + retest, then low-stakes tasks
* subcritical → recovery block, protect schedule
* in-zone/locked-in → do the hard work now

That’s actionable and measurable.

### C. It exposes dissociations that are common at work

Corporate reality: you can be physically OK but cognitively scattered (meetings, context switching), or physically depleted but mentally clear for a short window.

Your combined mind+body readout allows “smart allocation”:

* **Mind locked-in + Body subcritical** → deep work yes, but no intense training or late push
* **Mind spun-out + Body locked-in** → movement break + simple exercise, don’t do high-stakes cognition

Net effect: **less wasted time** and fewer days where strain accumulates unnoticed.

## 2) Cognitive longevity: consistency, recovery speed, and “protecting the brain’s learning zone”

### A. It prevents two failure modes that harm adherence

Longevity users often oscillate between:

* doing too much on “good days”
* doing nothing on “bad days”

Your four states create a **graded plan** that preserves habit continuity:

* subcritical → gentle recovery + light cognitive engagement
* spun-out → downshift protocols rather than piling on stimulation
* locked-in → higher-yield learning or training blocks
* in-zone → steady maintenance

That improves consistency, which is more important than hero sessions.

### B. It treats “high arousal” as two different biological situations

In longevity, “stress” is not always bad — *regulated* mobilisation can be healthy (training, engagement). Dysregulated mobilisation is the one that tends to correlate with poor sleep, rumination, and state drift.

Your fork:

* validates healthy challenge (locked-in)
* flags dysregulated “wired” states (spun-out) early, before they become weeks of poor recovery

### C. It makes “recovery” a measurable skill

Longevity isn’t just “time in the zone”, it’s **how fast you return** to it after disruption.

Because you separately label:

* subcritical (depleted)
* spun-out (dysregulated)
  …you can measure:
* frequency of each
* time-to-recover back to in-zone
* what interventions worked for *this person*

That’s a compelling longitudinal story for cognitive ageing: stability + resilience, not just raw performance.

## The unique advantage of doing it for both mind and body

The big differentiator is not “4 states” on its own — it’s:

### **4 mind states + 4 body states + dissociation handling**

Mainstream apps typically do one of these:

* physiology only → misses cognitive failure modes (spun-out productivity theatre)
* cognition only → misses recovery debt and overreach risk

Your combined model lets you say:

* “Your mind is ready but your body isn’t” (do deep work, protect recovery)
* “Your body is ready but your mind isn’t” (do simple physical training, avoid error-costly cognition)
* “Both are mobilised but dysregulated” (downshift, don’t stack intensity)

That translates into a product promise that fits both markets:

* **Corporate:** fewer preventable mistakes + less cumulative strain
* **Longevity:** steadier habits + faster recovery + safer training/load decisions
