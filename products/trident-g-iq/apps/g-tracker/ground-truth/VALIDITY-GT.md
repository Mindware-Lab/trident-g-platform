Psi-Band Cognitive Bandwidth Scale (Psi-CBS)
---

What it is: A brief self-report measure for tracking everyday cognitive performance over the past two weeks.

What the sections measure
Psi-CBS Core - Applied General Intelligence (G): Focus stability, error resistance, active processing, and learning/transfer in everyday work/study/project contexts.
Psi-CBS-AD - Cognitive Health & Resilience: Supplementary items tracking allostatic dysregulation / state-instability patterns.
Psi-CBS-AI - AI-Use Intelligence: Supplementary items tracking how AI tool use helps or hinders cognitive performance and workflow quality.
How it works
Responses use a 5-point frequency scale (Never to Very often).
The Core score is summarised from focus and processing subscales.
Some Core items are reverse-scored internally to maintain correct score direction.
Optional AD and AI sections provide additional pattern information rather than a diagnosis.
Current validation status (working summary)
Pilot evidence supports the intended structure and internal consistency of Psi-CBS, but current evidence is still limited (including smaller samples). Treat it as a practical tracking tool while further validation studies are ongoing.

Intended use
Self-tracking and product/research use
Tracking change over time (especially with consistent testing conditions)
Not a clinical or diagnostic assessment

Cognitive Resilience Scale (CRS-10)
---
What it is: A brief self-report questionnaire for tracking how well your thinking holds up under pressure over everyday life.

What it measures
CRS-10 focuses on cognitive resilience: your ability to maintain or regain effective thinking (focus, problem-solving, decision-making, learning) when stressed, facing setbacks, or working in high-stakes situations. It is deliberately about cognitive performance under stress, not general emotional resilience.

How it works
You respond to 10 statements on a 5-point agree/disagree scale. Some items reflect stress sharpening thinking (clarity, drive, mental "buzz"), while others reflect stress disrupting thinking (over-analysis, tunnel vision, reduced flexibility, demotivation). There is no time limit, and it typically takes 1-2 minutes to complete.

Current validation status (working summary)
CRS-10 has been used across multiple independent samples (including occupational and community contexts), and a translated validation study reports a clear single-factor structure with good reliability and expected links to related constructs (e.g., cognitive flexibility and resilience).

Intended use: Self-tracking, training/intervention evaluation, and product/research monitoring over time.
Not a clinical or diagnostic assessment.

Short g Scale (SgS-12)
---

What it is: A brief reasoning check that looks at how well you solve unfamiliar problems using patterns and simple rules.

What it measures
SgS-12 is designed to capture fluid reasoning. That is your ability to spot patterns, infer rules, and work things out when you have not seen the problem before.

How it works
You answer 12 multiple-choice questions. Items include things like pattern matrices, letter-number sequences, mental rotation, and a few short verbal reasoning questions. There are two versions (Form A and Form B) so you can repeat it later without seeing the exact same items.

Taking the test
There is no time limit, so it is about careful thinking rather than rushing. Most people finish in just a few minutes, but you can take a little longer if you need to.

Current validation status (working summary)
SgS-12 is built from public-domain items drawn from the International Cognitive Ability Resource (ICAR) test bank - specifically ICAR Matrix Reasoning items, plus the same ICAR family of Verbal Reasoning and Letter-Number Series item types, alongside ICAR's Three-Dimensional Rotation (R3D) items. These ICAR/R3D item sets have published psychometrics and large-sample norms, so the questions are not home-made puzzles but established item types with known difficulty and reliability characteristics. Because SgS-12 uses only 12 items, it is best treated as a practical reasoning snapshot and for tracking change over time, rather than as a definitive IQ test.

Intended use
Good for: self-tracking, baseline vs follow-up measurement in Trident G - IQ training, and product/research use.
Not for: clinical diagnosis or high-stakes decisions.

Everyday Decision Habits Scale (EDHS)
---
What it is: A brief, non-clinical questionnaire that helps you reflect on your everyday decision habits across common situations (work, health, money, planning, follow-through).

What it measures
EDHS captures four practical ingredients of effective self-regulation:

Planning & prioritisation (choosing what matters most rather than reacting)
Facing hard decisions vs avoidance (taking a concrete step on uncomfortable tasks)
Considering future consequences (balancing "now" with longer-term outcomes)
Concrete follow-through planning (turning intentions into specific plans, cues, and reminders)
How it works
You read a set of short real-life scenarios and pick the option (A-D) that best matches what you usually do. There are no right or wrong answers. EDHS includes two versions (A and B) so it can be repeated over time without using the exact same situations.

Current validation status (working summary)
EDHS is research-informed by design, with its four habit areas adapted from established work on decision-making styles, present-vs-future focus, and planning/implementation habits. Because it is brief, it is best treated as a practical snapshot for self-reflection and tracking patterns over time, rather than a definitive assessment.

Intended use: Self-tracking, training/intervention evaluation, and product/research monitoring of habit change.
Not a clinical or diagnostic tool.

Mensa Online - long (25 minutes)
---
Online matrix IQ test for baseline indication. Developed by Mensa Denmark and Mensa Norway.

When: Optional baseline only.

Credentials: Useful as an optional benchmark with widely recognised validity, but not a formal professional IQ assessment.
