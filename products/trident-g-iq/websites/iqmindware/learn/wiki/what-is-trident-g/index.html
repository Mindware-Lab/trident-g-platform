What Is Trident-G? Architecture Summary

Trident-G is a domain-general intelligence architecture. It treats intelligence as a usable range: the capacity to stay viable, move across exploration and exploitation, coordinate banked knowledge with live reasoning, and turn what survives real use back into reusable competence.

Reviewed:2026-04-04
By:Mark Ashton Smith
Topic:General intelligence architecture and Trident-G summary

What Trident-G is

Trident-G is best read as an operating architecture rather than a single drill, score, or narrow theory. It describes how a system stays viable under load, reasons through novelty, stabilises useful structure, and banks what worked into lower-cost reusable competence.

In plain language, the model says good cognition is not just raw problem-solving. It is the ability to keep functioning cleanly enough to switch between flexible reasoning and dependable execution, then carry useful structure forward into future tasks.

The trident structure

The summary document describes Trident-G as a shaft-and-tines architecture:

  • Shaft = Gc: banked, reusable support structure such as validated scripts, operator chains, compressed gist, and crystallised competence.
  • Centre tine = Gf: live relational reasoning that keeps task structure active long enough to compare, transform, test, compress, and commit.
  • Outer tines: exploration, which widens possibility space, and exploitation, which tightens control and mutual information around a workable route.

The centre tine matters because it bridges multiple layers at once: viability control and banked Gc, exploration and exploitation, and live flexible reasoning with the working-memory and attention-control substrate that keeps relevant structure online.

What the "G" means

In this framework, G should not be reduced to fluid intelligence alone. Trident-G uses a broader definition: the overall usable range of general intelligence across which the system can stay viable, shift adaptively, and coordinate banked Gc with live Gf.

Gc-dominant cognition is the lower-cost, more automated end of that range: installed scripts, validated concepts, rapid retrieval, and reliable execution. Gf-dominant cognition is the higher-challenge end: active restructuring, relational comparison, probing, reframing, and model revision under novelty.

The broader claim is architectural, not mystical: intelligence is the capacity to work across that range without losing corridor control, then crystallise what survives back into reusable knowledge and skill.

The two meta-missions

The summary places two meta-missions above the applied layers of the system:

  • Viability: maintain adaptive allostasis inside the defended psi corridor so learning, inference, and action remain clean and recoverable.
  • Expansion: enlarge viable dynamic range and portable operator repertoire through exploration, exploitation, gist extraction, and crystallisation into reusable Gc.

These are not just labels for a diagram. They are trainable global orientations. Viability means preferring recoverable, in-band functioning over brittle overreach. Expansion means enlarging real reasoning range and portable repertoire rather than chasing wrapper-bound success.

Mission frames, control missions, and meta-dials

Beneath the meta-missions, the summary separates several applied layers that govern how the system is used in practice:

  • Mission frames: Understand, Argue, Choose, Plan and Do, Learn.
  • Control missions: shield attention, regulate cadence, prioritise, create distance, calibrate mode, arbitrate script vs improvisation, recover, re-enter, externalise, or escalate.
  • Meta-dials: slower governance variables such as meta-efficacy, coherence, compression depth, complexity governance, subjective calibration, proof discipline, exploration-exploitation governance, and ecology support.
  • Feedback signals: fast diagnostic cues telling the loop to simplify, probe, recover, update, or restructure.

The point of this layering is not to create separate theories. It is to clarify what the whole system is trying to become, what a loop is being run for right now, what keeps that loop viable, and what slower variables shape performance across time and context.

The integrated stack and the G-Loop

The summary then describes an integrated stack rather than isolated modules. Trident-G includes mission frames, a language of thought and action, a reasoning calculus, semantic and syntax layers, a subjective interface, an allostatic viability layer, a cognitive runtime, and a fast relational runtime.

The G-Loop remains the shared kernel running through those layers. It is the smallest repeatable cycle that keeps cognition viable while mapping, testing, committing, updating, compressing, and banking what survives real use.

That matters because operator algebra is only one part of the picture. The loop also depends on semantics, subjectivity, state control, feedback signals, and runtime conditions. In other words, Trident-G is not just a list of operators. It is a full architecture for viable inference, action, and reusable learning.

FAQ

Is Trident-G just fluid intelligence training?

No. The summary explicitly says Trident-G should not be reduced to Gf alone. It is about the viable range across Gc, Gf, exploration, exploitation, and the governance needed to move between them cleanly.

What is the difference between Gf and Gc in Trident-G?

Gc is the banked support structure: validated scripts, concepts, and reusable competence. Gf is the live relational bridge used when novelty, challenge, or restructuring demand active reasoning.

Why does Trident-G put viability before expansion?

Because learning and carryover become brittle when the system is outside a workable regime. Viability protects clean operation first; expansion then grows range and repertoire from a stable base.
