# Trident-G IQ Public Website QA Checklist

## Purpose
Use this checklist to review any public-facing webpage for Trident-G IQ, including homepage, app pages, coaching pages, pricing pages, proof pages, FAQs, and landing pages.

The aim is simple: make pages clear, trustworthy, commercially effective, and easy for a general adult reader to scan without feeling overwhelmed by technicality, jargon, or too much content.

---

## Core standard
Every public page should be:

- clear enough for a thoughtful general reader
- rigorous enough for a science-based brand
- restrained enough to stay credible
- simple enough to scan quickly on first visit

---

## Pass rule
A page should pass only if:

- all **Critical checks** pass
- at least **12 of 16** standard checks pass
- there are **no red-flag failures**

---

## Critical checks
These must all pass.

- [ ] **The page clearly says what it is about near the top**
- [ ] **The page is written mainly in plain English**
- [ ] **The page has one main purpose**
- [ ] **The page does not make exaggerated, guaranteed, or medical-style claims**
- [ ] **The page has one clear next step**

---

## Standard checks

### 1) Clarity and first impression
- [ ] A first-time visitor can understand the page within a few seconds
- [ ] The headline and opening section explain the page in simple terms
- [ ] The opening focuses on user need or benefit, not theory
- [ ] The most important message appears before any deep explanation

### 2) Scannability
- [ ] The page uses clear headings and short sections
- [ ] Paragraphs are short and readable
- [ ] The page is easy to skim without reading every line
- [ ] Key points are not buried in long blocks of text

### 3) Complexity control
- [ ] Jargon, acronyms, and internal model language are avoided or briefly explained
- [ ] Technical detail is kept light on public sales or landing pages
- [ ] Deeper science, protocol, or theory is linked out rather than crammed into the page

### 4) Trust and credibility
- [ ] The tone is calm, factual, and non-hyped
- [ ] Claims are modest and believable
- [ ] The page clearly separates evidence, theory, and aspiration
- [ ] Testimonials, if used, do not imply claims that cannot be supported

### 5) Action and flow
- [ ] The page ends with one obvious next action
- [ ] Buttons and links are specific, for example `See pricing`, `View protocol`, or `Book a call`

---

## Red-flag failures
If any of these are present, the page fails.

- [ ] The page feels dense, academic, or overly technical for a general audience
- [ ] The page leads with theory instead of what the user gets or does
- [ ] The page tries to do too many jobs at once
- [ ] The page includes unexplained specialist terms
- [ ] The page sounds pushy, inflated, or sensational
- [ ] The page implies diagnosis, treatment, cure, or guaranteed outcomes
- [ ] The page has no clear call to action
- [ ] The page contains large walls of text with little structure

---

## House rules for Trident-G IQ pages

### Must do
- [ ] Say what the product, page, or offer is in plain English near the top
- [ ] Lead with user benefit, use-case, or practical value
- [ ] Keep theory secondary on general public pages
- [ ] Use a calm and credible tone
- [ ] Make the next step obvious
- [ ] Add safety or scope wording where needed

### Must avoid
- [ ] Long theoretical explanations on homepage, pricing, or sales pages
- [ ] Dense neuroscience language unless the page is specifically for proof or protocol
- [ ] Over-claiming what the apps or coaching can do
- [ ] Language that sounds medical unless intentionally and properly framed
- [ ] Vague links such as repeated `Learn more` with no clear distinction

---

## Simple score
Use this if you want a quick decision.

- **Pass** = all Critical checks pass, no red flags, and 12-16 standard checks pass
- **Borderline** = all Critical checks pass, no red flags, and 9-11 standard checks pass
- **Fail** = any Critical check fails, any red flag appears, or fewer than 9 standard checks pass

---

## Quick reviewer prompts
Use these when auditing a page.

1. What is this page trying to help the visitor do?
2. Would a non-specialist understand it quickly?
3. Is the first screen useful, or does it make the visitor work too hard?
4. Is there too much theory for the page type?
5. Does anything sound exaggerated or unsafe?
6. Is the next step obvious?

## Suggested use in repo
Apply this checklist to:

- homepage
- app overview pages
- coaching pages
- pricing pages
- FAQ pages
- proof pages

Suggested workflow:

1. Review the page against this checklist
2. Mark pass, borderline, or fail
3. Revise weak sections
4. Re-check before publishing

## One-line reminder
**Public pages should invite confidence and action, not demand study.**
