## 1) Purpose of this document

This file is the editorial ground truth for the IQ Mindware Substack.

Its job is to keep future Substack content aligned with the live IQ Mindware website, public proof posture, and Trident-G / IQ Mindware brand logic. It exists to constrain drift, prevent overclaiming, and make sure both free and paid content reinforce the same central promise.

The Substack should not become:
- a generic AI newsletter
- a generic productivity newsletter
- a vague “self-optimisation” publication
- a neuroscience-heavy theory dump
- a hype vehicle for unsupported IQ or far-transfer claims

It **should** function as:
- a public lab for transferable intelligence
- a clear explanation layer for IQ Mindware and Trident-G
- a commentary layer on AI, cognition, and the future of work
- a proof-first research and synthesis layer
- a practical application layer for paid members

## 3) Claims posture (must govern all Substack content)

### Safe claims
The Substack may say that IQ Mindware / Trident-G is:
- designed to train **general intelligence capacity** and **cognitive resilience**
- designed to train **portable thinking skills** and **mindware scripts**
- designed to **test carryover under changed conditions**
- designed around **game swaps, boundary checks, delayed re-checks, and mission use**
- committed to **public protocols**, **aggregated summaries**, and **clear claims boundaries**
- focused on **skills training and self-regulation**, not diagnosis or treatment

### Red-line claims
Do **not** say:
- guaranteed far transfer
- guaranteed IQ increase
- clinically proven, unless tied to specific cited evidence and used narrowly
- cures, treats, diagnoses, or prevents medical conditions
- universal outcome claims based on testimonials
- “brain-criticality proves this works”
- “the neuroscience proves it”
- anything implying certainty beyond the actual evidence

### House rule
**Proof language must always be stronger than outcome language.**
It is acceptable to say:
- “designed to”
- “aims to”
- “tests whether”
- “checks whether”
- “transfer checked, not assumed”
- “evidence base still growing”
- “outcomes vary”

It is not acceptable to write as though transfer has already been fully settled across users.

## 10) Tone and style

### Tone
Use a voice that is:
- calm
- rigorous
- commercially literate
- practical
- reflective
- proof-first
- older-friendly
- plain-spoken

### Avoid
- breathless hype
- tech-bro posturing
- doom-mongering
- inflated confidence
- excessive jargon
- dense theory for its own sake
- making every piece sound like a sales page

### Style rule
Lead with a real problem or tension.
Then connect it to:
1. the framework
2. the mechanism
3. the evidence or reasoning
4. the practical implication

### Practical language preference
Prefer:
- “carry over”
- “tested under change”
- “portable thinking skills”
- “reasoning under load”
- “usable strategy”
- “better judgement”
- “cleaner execution”
- “state drift”
- “human-led AI”

Over:
- “cognitive enhancement” as the main phrase
- “peak performance” clichés
- “biohacking”
- “limitless”
- “rewire your brain”
- “supercharge”
- “hack your IQ”

## 11) How theory should appear

### Theory is allowed, but in service of clarity
The Substack may discuss:
- Trident-G theory
- free energy
- active inference
- near-criticality / Ψ-band ideas
- operator portability
- thin automation
- relational structure
- extended cognition

But:
- theory should illuminate a concrete problem
- theory should be translated into plain language
- theory should not dominate most pieces
- theory must not be used as a shortcut to overclaiming

### Good use of theory
Use theory to answer questions like:
- Why do many brain-training gains fail to carry over?
- Why does state matter for learning quality?
- Why might portable scripts matter more than raw repetition?
- How can AI help without replacing judgement?
- Why do speed and overload often degrade reasoning?
