## 1. Purpose

The **H-AGI Loop** is a human-led protocol for using AI in a way that supports, rather than replaces, intelligence.

Its purpose is not simply to improve prompt quality or accelerate task completion. Its purpose is to help a person:

- preserve cognitive ownership
- improve reasoning under load
- sharpen deep relational mapping
- extract reusable rules or scripts from interaction
- reapply those rules under changed conditions
- verify what genuinely carries over

In IQ Mindware terms, the loop is a practical AI-facing expression of the **Trident G** method:

- **Zone first**
- **Gf -> Gc -> Gf**
- **train the operator, not just the prompt**
- **bank what survives checks**
- **prove carryover under changed conditions**

---

## 2. Core claim

The H-AGI Loop rests on one central claim:

> AI should help the human sharpen the map, bank the rule, and reapply it under change — without taking over the first-pass organising role of intelligence.

This means AI is treated as:

- a refiner
- a challenger
- a contrastive engine
- a scaffold
- a provisional thought partner

It is **not** treated as the owner of the answer, the source of final judgement, or the substitute for first-pass reasoning.

## 4. Mode selection

The loop is graded. Not every task deserves the same depth of engagement.

Before starting, choose the appropriate mode.

### Fast
**Quick execution, minimal learning loop**

Use when:
- the task is low stakes
- speed matters more than learning
- little judgement is required
- you are comfortable offloading most of the work
- there is little value in extracting a reusable script

Typical examples:
- routine summaries
- administrative drafting
- travel or scheduling assistance
- formatting or clean-up tasks
- quick idea generation with low consequences

Expected behaviour:
- light state check
- limited ownership
- minimal audit
- no serious script banking
- no formal proof check

### Assisted
**Moderate oversight, partial loop**

Use when:
- the task has some value but is not deeply strategic
- you want support without surrendering full ownership
- some judgement matters
- there may be moderate learning value
- you want to preserve reasoning quality without running the full loop

Typical examples:
- meeting preparation
- first-pass memo structuring
- article scaffolding
- moderate-stakes comparison tasks
- bounded drafting or synthesis

Expected behaviour:
- Zone check
- human first-pass framing
- AI-supported refinement
- lighter stress-testing
- active audit
- brief rewrite in your own words
- optional capture of a useful rule

### G-engaged
**Full reasoning + learning loop**

Use when:
- the task is high stakes
- the topic is central to your work or growth
- you want to learn the structure, not just finish the task
- you want a portable script or operator
- judgement quality matters
- you care whether the gain will carry over under changed conditions

Typical examples:
- strategy or product decisions
- research interpretation
- argument construction
- high-value writing
- mission planning
- complex comparisons under uncertainty
- skill-building tasks where transfer matters

Expected behaviour:
- full Zone-first engagement
- human first-pass map
- structured refinement
- stress-testing
- deliberate audit
- explicit rewrite and script extraction
- reapplication in a new wrapper
- proof check

## 5. Step-by-step ground truth

## Step 1. Zone
**Check state before deep interaction**

The loop starts with the operator, not the model.

The question is:
- am I in a workable state for reasoning?
- am I overloaded, scattered, flat, impulsive, or too hurried?
- will my current state increase drift, premature closure, or lazy offloading?

### Why it matters
Poor state increases:
- cognitive drift
- passive inheritance of machine framing
- weak evaluation
- brittle reasoning
- low-quality script formation

### In Trident G terms
This is the **Zone gate**.  
Good AI use depends on operator quality.  
If state is poor, either:
- retune
- reduce the task scope
- switch to a lower-engagement mode
- postpone high-value reasoning

### Typical prompts to self
- What state am I in?
- Is this a task for deep reasoning or quick execution?
- Should I recover or simplify before starting?

---

## Step 2. Own
**Create the first map yourself**

Before asking AI to solve the problem, the human should create an initial structure.

This usually includes:
- the problem in your own words
- a provisional answer or direction
- two to four uncertainties that genuinely matter
- what success would look like

### Why it matters
Once the machine frames the problem for you, you are already downstream of its assumptions.

### In Trident G terms
This is the beginning of the **Gf phase**:
- first-pass mapping
- uncertainty holding
- relational framing
- task ownership

### Good outputs from this step
- a rough decision frame
- a first argument skeleton
- a first hypothesis map
- a mission frame
- a question set worth refining

---

## Step 3. Refine
**Use AI to sharpen the frame, not to own the answer**

At this stage AI is invited in, but not as oracle.

Its role is to:
- improve distinctions
- sharpen uncertainties
- suggest missing variables
- expose hidden assumptions
- turn vague concerns into stronger questions

### Why it matters
Better reasoning often begins with a better question set, not a faster answer.

### In Trident G terms
This remains part of the **Gf phase**:
- discrimination
- reframing
- deepening the relational structure
- improving the quality of the map

### Good AI roles here
- question refiner
- contrastive engine
- assumption exposer
- structure clarifier

### Bad AI role here
- final answer generator before you have done your own framing

---

## Step 4. Stress-test
**Challenge the draft before you trust it**

Once there is a draft answer, model, plan, or argument, AI is used to create productive tension around it.

The most useful modes are usually:
- **Socratic mode**: ask questions that force clarification
- **Devil's Advocate mode**: challenge assumptions, trade-offs, and failure modes

### Why it matters
This protects against:
- premature closure
- seduction by fluency
- under-specified reasoning
- untested assumptions

### In Trident G terms
This is still part of the **Gf phase**:
- discriminating between possibilities
- evaluating competing views
- surfacing fragility
- improving operator judgement

### Good outputs from this step
- clearer trade-offs
- explicit thresholds
- better decision rules
- stronger argument structure
- awareness of what could falsify the current view

---

## Step 5. Audit
**Judge the machine instead of admiring it**

This is the step many people skip.

The human now evaluates the AI output:
- what is genuinely strong?
- what is vague or generic?
- what sounds plausible but unsupported?
- what assumptions has the model made?
- what important perspective is missing?
- what still requires evidence?

### Why it matters
AI output that sounds good is not the same thing as reasoning that is good.

### In Trident G terms
This completes the major **Gf phase**:
- evaluate structure
- assess support
- identify missing conditions
- decide what is worth keeping

### Protected capacities
This step especially protects:
- epistemic integrity
- judgement
- self-monitoring
- interpretation

---

## Step 6. Re-write / Bank
**Return to your own words and extract the rule**

The human now comes back out of the machine.

Write:
- the revised answer in your own words
- what changed
- what stayed stable
- what rule, heuristic, script, or operator is worth keeping

This is the point where the interaction becomes useful beyond the current task.

### Why it matters
The goal is not only a better answer now.  
The goal is a reusable **mindware script**.

### In Trident G terms
This is **Gc extraction**:
- compiling what worked
- compressing it into portable form
- creating a reusable script that can later be applied again

### Good script examples
- a decision threshold rule
- a better framing routine
- a challenge prompt that reliably exposes weak assumptions
- a rewrite habit that restores judgement
- a domain-specific strategy rule

---

## Step 7. Reapply
**Use the script again in a new wrapper**

A script is only valuable if it can be used beyond the original interaction.

Reapply it:
- in a new task
- under different stakes
- in a different context
- with different surface content
- under mild pressure or novelty

### Why it matters
This is where the loop returns from **Gc back into Gf**.

The script is not just stored. It is tested in a new mission.

### In Trident G terms
This is **Gf return under change**.

The user moves from:
- one-off insight
to
- flexible reapplication under changed conditions

---

## Step 8. Proof
**Check whether it genuinely carried over**

Do not assume transfer. Check it.

Possible proof checks include:
- **wrapper swap**: does it still work in a changed surface form?
- **boundary/trap probe**: where does it fail?
- **delayed re-check**: does it still hold later?
- **mission success signal**: did it improve a real outcome?

### Why it matters
This is what separates:
- a pleasant interaction
from
- a portable and trustworthy operator gain

### In Trident G terms
Only what survives checks should be treated as bankable.

### Practical rule
**What holds gets reused. What fails gets revised.**

---

## 6. Deep structure of the loop

The H-AGI Loop is not only an interaction protocol. It is a learning loop.

### The deeper structure is:
**Zone -> Gf -> Gc -> Gf -> Proof**

### Expanded form
- **Zone**: enter from a workable state
- **Gf**: map, refine, discriminate, stress-test, evaluate
- **Gc**: bank the rule as a mindware script
- **Gf again**: apply the script under novelty or changed conditions
- **Proof**: verify whether it carried over

This is what makes the loop distinctively **Trident G**.

## 7. Core editorial and practical claims

The H-AGI Loop is built on the following claims.

### 1. AI should not own the first map
The human should frame the first structure of the problem whenever the task matters.

### 2. Good AI use depends on operator quality
Poor state, weak self-monitoring, and unstable capacity increase bad offloading.

### 3. The value of AI is often in refinement and challenge
Its best role is often to sharpen, contrast, expose, and stress-test — not to replace the human answer.

### 4. Rewriting matters
The final answer should come back into the human's own words.

### 5. The real gain is script formation
The aim is not one good interaction, but a reusable operator rule.

### 6. Carryover must be checked
Transfer is checked, not assumed.

## 9. Practical usage rule

Use the H-AGI Loop whenever you want AI to support intelligence without replacing it.

### Use Fast mode when:
- the task is routine
- the stakes are low
- speed matters more than learning

### Use Assisted mode when:
- the task matters somewhat
- you want support with oversight
- you want to preserve some ownership

- the task is important
- the learning value is high
- you care about judgement
- you want to bank a script
- you want to know whether something truly carries over

## 13. What this is not

The H-AGI Loop is not:

- a generic prompting framework
- a productivity hack
- a case for replacing thought with model output
- a transhuman fusion model
- a claim that AI should dissolve the human first-pass role
- a guarantee that every AI-assisted interaction improves intelligence

It is a **human-led control and learning sequence**.
