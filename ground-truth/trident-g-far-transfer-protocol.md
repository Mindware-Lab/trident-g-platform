## Canonical Trident-G far-transfer protocol

### X0. Purpose

Trident-G addresses far transfer by training two things together: a reusable inference-and-validation system and the capacity to run it reliably under load within a defended Ψ corridor. Portability is not assumed. It is tested through syntax swaps, wrapper swaps, boundary checks, stake variation, and cue-fired real-world deployment, and is banked only when it survives those tests. 

In this framework, far transfer means **portable operator reliability**: a learned operator, tactic, or script can survive changed surface form, still improve performance for the right reason, and fire from real-world cues across time and context rather than only inside its original training wrapper. 

### X1. Core claim

The canonical Trident-G far-transfer protocol is organised by **two training dimensions under one viability constraint**.

The **vertical dimension** moves the learner upward from state-readiness to runtime support, explicit reasoning, bounded problem-space rehearsal, real mission deployment, and finally script banking. The **horizontal dimension** applies selection pressure across changed tokens, syntaxes, spatial frames, and semantic wrappers while holding the underlying invariant as constant as possible. The **Ψ / Zone condition** cuts across both, because out-of-band learning contaminates credit assignment and teaches state survival rather than portable structure.  

So the protocol is not just “train and hope”. It is: **stabilise → perturb → select invariants → deploy → bank**, with viability protected throughout. 

### X2. The cross-cutting Zone rule

For the canonical protocol, being sufficiently **in the zone** is not merely the first step. It is the **validity condition for learning across the whole stack**. The documents are clear that learning should only be intensified when the learner is sufficiently in band, because high load, high drift, or repeated re-entry failure corrupt clean updating. When that happens, the correct move is recovery, simplification, pacing change, or externalisation first.  

So Zone is best understood in two ways at once: as the **entry gate** to a session, and as a **continuous constraint** on both vertical progression and horizontal variation. A learner can only claim stronger far-transfer evidence if the operator survives not just across wrappers, but across wrappers while the loop remains sufficiently viable to support clean inference and clean credit assignment. 

### X3. Vertical dimension: where transfer is meant to travel

The vertical axis is the main applied transfer path:

**Zone → Capacity → Reasoning → Problem Space / Mission Bootcamp → Real Mission → Script / Bank**. 

#### V1. Zone / viability gate

Zone establishes whether the learner is trainable now. Its role is not to teach reasoning directly, but to protect valid learning conditions. If the learner is overheated, flat, drifting, or unstable, the protocol should simplify, reduce dose, recover, or postpone rather than force heavy training.  

#### V2. Capacity / runtime substrate

Capacity training strengthens the substrate that makes later reasoning runnable under load: holding, updating, shielding, interference resistance, re-entry, relational binding, and related control functions. In Trident-G terms this is the main **Type-1 widening and stabilisation** layer. It improves the odds that later reasoning and deployment remain clean rather than collapsing under drift or lure capture.  

#### V3. Reasoning / explicit inferential layer

Reasoning drills make the formal layer explicit. Here the learner practises probe selection, constraint propagation, working-hypothesis stabilisation, next-move choice, and gist extraction. This is where tactics and strategies become visible rather than remaining implicit. It bridges runtime support and structured problem solving.  

#### V4. Problem space / bounded mission world

The problem-space layer recombines capacity and reasoning inside a bounded world. It is the small-world rehearsal layer in which tactics, strategies, and relational holding are coupled to action under constraints. Its job is not yet full ecological deployment, but mission-proximal structured rehearsal.  

#### V5. Real mission / cue-fired deployment

This is where far transfer is actually tested. The learner applies the trained structure in a live mission frame such as **Understand, Argue, Choose, or Plan & Do**, with Learn operating as the acquisition orientation across those frames. Real mission success outranks local practice reward because ecological success is the strongest reinforcement signal in the far-transfer architecture.  

#### V6. Script / Bank

The endpoint is not raw memory of the session but conversion into reusable competence. What gets banked is the portable compression of what survived: the cue, the steps or operators, the check, the boundaries or traps, and the stop rule. Banking is conservative because one-off success, fluency, or a pleasant click is not enough. 

### X4. Horizontal dimension: how thin automation is prevented

The horizontal axis is the anti-thin-automation discipline. Its core rule is:

**hold the deep structure constant while varying the surface code**.  

In practical terms, the learner should face the **same deep skill** across altered token sets, syntaxes, mappings, semantic wrappers, and spatial frames. This is strongest in Capacity Gym, lighter in Reasoning, implicit in Puzzle / Problem Space work, and proven in live mission use. 

At the capacity layer, the documents are especially clear: the block should preserve the same underlying kernel while varying the wrapper. The learner is not meant merely to see “new stimuli”, but to preserve the same invariant across changed presentation. 

A canonical staircase for the horizontal axis is:

* fixed concrete wrapper
* remapped but structurally identical wrapper
* concept-level wrapper
* syntax swap across verbal / visuospatial / numerical form
* broader semantic wrapper swap into mission-related context.  

This matches the broader architecture, where syntax is a surface coding layer, not the deep structure itself. Syntax swaps are therefore a controlled subtype of wrapper swap used to test whether the same semantic target and operator demand survive a change in surface code. 

### X5. How the two dimensions combine

The two dimensions should not be treated as separate programmes.

The **vertical question** is:
Can this skill travel from viable training conditions to real-world mission use?

The **horizontal question** is:
Can this skill survive changes in syntax, tokens, mappings, and wrapper without collapsing into surface-bound performance?

Only when both answers are positive do we have stronger evidence of genuine far transfer. And even then, that evidence is only trustworthy if the learner remained sufficiently in band for clean updating rather than merely surviving an out-of-zone state.  

### X6. Canonical session logic

A clean canonical session can therefore be stated as follows.

First, **gate by Zone**. If the learner is not sufficiently in band, recover or simplify first. Second, set the current **mission frame** so the loop has a defined purpose. Third, run a **capacity block** that strengthens runtime control while applying horizontal wrapper variation. Fourth, run **reasoning drills** that preserve logical form while varying scenarios. Fifth, run a **bounded problem-space task** that recombines runtime support and tactics in one coherent structured world. Sixth, run a **real mission** in life so cue-fired deployment is tested outside the app. Seventh, **probe, validate, and bank** only what survives portability checks and real or strongly mission-proximal success.  

### X7. Evidence thresholds

The protocol should distinguish between **transfer observed** and **reliable far transfer**.

Transfer observed is the minimum proof-of-life level: the candidate survives at least one wrapper swap and is enacted at least once in a real situation. Reliable far transfer is stronger: at least two distinct wrapper swaps, at least three cue-fired deployments across at least two days, some boundary discrimination for tempting near-misses, and stability under mild load variation. 

This is important because syntax robustness alone is not the whole of far transfer. It is an earlier portability threshold, not the final one. Real deployment and conservative banking still matter. 

### X8. Routing and adaptation rules

When the cue does not fire, the problem should be treated as a retrieval or trigger issue, not immediately as lack of ability. When the cue fires but the operator fails, the problem is more likely one of portability, script-fit, or boundary weakness. When drills look good but performance collapses under pressure, the diagnosis should shift toward state stability, corridor control, and insufficient Type-1 robustness. When mismatch becomes unexpectedly unstable and re-entry keeps failing, the protocol should escalate from tuning into restructuring. 

This means the protocol is adaptive not only in difficulty, but in **diagnosis of failure type**. The question is not simply “did the learner get it right?”, but “what kind of failure occurred, at what layer, and under what state conditions?” 

### X9. Canonical one-paragraph synthesis

Trident-G far transfer is best defined as a **Ψ-gated two-axis protocol**. Vertically, it moves the learner from viability through capacity support, explicit reasoning, bounded problem-space rehearsal, real mission deployment, and script banking. Horizontally, it varies syntax, wrapper, and surface form while preserving deep structure so that gains are selected for invariance rather than thin automation. Zone cuts across both dimensions because out-of-band learning corrupts clean credit assignment and portability selection. Real mission outcomes outrank local practice reward, and only structures that survive probe, validation, wrapper variation, cue-fired deployment, and delayed re-check are banked into Gc as reusable competence.   

### X10. Scope note

This protocol is for supporting healthy performance and more reliable intelligent action. It should not be framed as medical treatment or as a guaranteed outcome system. If users are concerned about sleep, mood, anxiety, pain, or related health issues, they should consult their GP.
