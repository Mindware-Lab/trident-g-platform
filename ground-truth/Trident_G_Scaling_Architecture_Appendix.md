## Appendix Y. Scaling architecture for Trident-G training programmes

### Y0. Purpose

This appendix sets out how Trident-G training programmes should scale across multiple products, mission families, and domain-specific packs without fragmenting the underlying architecture. The aim is to preserve one common Trident-G engine while allowing different user-facing experiences, training emphases, syntaxes, wrappers, telemetry schemes, and commercial packages.

The core rule is simple:

**The kernel stays the same. The foreground mission family, operator emphasis, reasoning emphasis, capacity emphasis, syntax and semantic wrappers, and proof schema can change.**

This is consistent with the source architecture. Trident-G uses a single G-Loop kernel, the same shared training stack, and the same two top meta-missions of **Viability** and **Expansion**, while different mission families foreground different targets rather than relying on separate underlying mechanisms.

### Y1. Core claim

Trident-G should be scaled as a **family of training programmes built on one shared engine**, not as a set of unrelated theories or disconnected apps.

At the deepest level, all scaled versions of Trident-G share:

- the same G-Loop kernel
- the same language of thought and action
- the same reasoning calculus
- the same broad cognitive runtime and capacity substrate
- the same Ψ-allostatic viability logic
- the same far-transfer rule that portability must be tested rather than assumed
- the same banking rule that only validated scripts become Gc

What changes across scaled products is not the kernel, but the **mission foreground**, the **training emphasis**, the **syntax and semantic wrapper library**, and the **telemetry or proof layer**. This fits the G-Loop claim that the purpose can change while the kernel does not, and the missions framework claim that the two mission families differ mainly in which target is foregrounded.

### Y2. What remains fixed across the scaled family

#### Y2.1 Meta-missions

The highest-level meta-missions remain the same across all Trident-G products:

**Viability**  
Maintain adaptive operation within the defended Ψ corridor so learning, inference, and action remain clean and recoverable.

**Expansion**  
Enlarge viable dynamic range and portable operator repertoire through the coordination of exploration, exploitation, and crystallisation into reusable Gc.

These meta-missions are not product-specific. They apply to the whole architecture.

#### Y2.2 Shared kernel

Every Trident-G programme should continue to run the same kernel logic:

- enter or restore workable corridor occupancy
- set mission frame
- allocate rigour through salience
- build the simplest usable map
- infer through the live relational centre
- test by probe and validation
- commit through satisficing
- act, observe, and update
- bank only what survives portability checks

#### Y2.3 Shared formal layer

Every scaled programme should preserve the same high-level formal architecture:

**Language of thought and action**  
= operator algebra + mission grammar

**Reasoning calculus**  
= inferential transformations run within that language

The operator algebra remains shared because the same primitive operators are designed to work across mission frames, wrappers, and domains. The reasoning calculus also remains shared because deductive, inductive, abductive, analogical, Bayesian, and value-of-information reasoning are cross-cutting inference styles rather than separate domain-specific logics.

#### Y2.4 Shared syntax logic

Every scaled programme should also preserve the same syntax logic.

The high-level architecture defines syntax as the **surface representational form** of the same deeper structure. The same semantic target and operator demand may therefore be expressed in verbal, visuospatial, or numerical form, where applicable. This means syntax is not the same thing as mission grammar. Mission grammar governs well-formed operator sequencing. Syntax governs surface rendering.

A **syntax swap** is best treated as a specific subtype of wrapper swap: a controlled change in representational modality while keeping the deeper semantic target and operator demand as constant as possible.

#### Y2.5 Shared runtime and viability substrate

Every scaled programme should preserve the same broad substrate:

- working memory
- attention management
- control stability
- interference resistance
- re-entry quality
- viable computation under load

The same Ψ-allostatic logic also remains fixed: high load or high drift routes to simplification, recovery, or externalisation before stronger conclusions are drawn, and mismatch or unexpected mismatch governs updating versus restructuring.

#### Y2.6 Shared far-transfer rule

Every scaled programme must preserve the same far-transfer discipline:

- portability is not assumed
- wrapper swaps, including syntax swaps, stake swaps, boundary checks, and cue-fired deployment are required
- local reward is not sufficient for banking
- only validated scripts become Gc

The source docs are explicit that wrapper swaps are the mechanism that tests whether gains preserve **deep invariants rather than surface bindings**. Validation should therefore hold deep operator demand constant while varying surface form, context, or stakes.

### Y3. What can vary safely across scaled programmes

Scaling does not require a new kernel. It requires controlled variation in the right places.

The following can change safely across products or packs:

- the **foreground mission family**
- the **operator bundle most often trained**
- the **reasoning styles most often emphasised**
- the **capacity targets most heavily trained**
- the **syntax library** used most often
- the **semantic wrapper library**
- the **subjective and coaching emphasis**
- the **telemetry and proof schema**
- the **threshold for useful improvement**
- the **user-facing framing and branding**

This is the correct level of customisation. It allows one theory to scale into multiple programmes without theoretical fragmentation.

### Y4. The two primary applied mission families

The cleanest top-level scaling move is to preserve two primary applied mission families built on one shared kernel.

#### Y4.1 IQ / Performance family

This family foregrounds:

- Understand
- Argue
- Choose
- Plan & Do
- Learn

This is the family for portable reasoning, decision quality, strategic execution, and acquisition of reusable competence. Mission frames in this family optimise for better maps, stronger arguments, better decisions, cleaner execution, and successful learning of transferable scripts.

#### Y4.2 Resilience / Self-regulation family

This family foregrounds:

- Shield attention
- Regulate cadence
- Prioritise missions
- Create distance
- Calibrate mode
- Arbitrate improvisation vs script
- Recover / re-enter
- Externalise / escalate

This is the family for keeping the loop viable, stable, and trainable under distraction, fatigue, overload, emotional pressure, and unstable salience. These are not separate reasoning frames. They are outer-loop control missions that protect credit assignment and preserve in-band execution.

#### Y4.3 Why they must still feel like one family

These two mission families should not be presented as separate theories. The source documents are clear that:

- both families draw on the same shared training stack
- capacity training strengthens the stability and flexibility needed to run the loop under load
- mindware training specifies the operator chains, probes, and scripts that the loop runs
- self-regulation missions are not mindware-free and can be built from the same operator set rather than a second algebra

So the correct product interpretation is:

**same meta-missions, same kernel, same language of thought and action, different applied mission family foregrounded**

### Y5. The training stack that should scale across products

The most useful scaling unit is not a single game or single app. It is a **shared training stack**.

#### Y5.1 Zone / viability gate

Every scaled programme should begin with zone logic:

- Am I in band?
- Am I overheated, flat, drifting, or unstable?
- Should I train, simplify, recover, or externalise first?

This remains non-negotiable because out-of-band training contaminates credit assignment and teaches survival to state rather than portability of structure.

#### Y5.2 Capacity training

Capacity training strengthens the substrate that makes the language of thought and action runnable under load. Depending on the programme, this may foreground:

- relational holding
- updating
- switching
- interference resistance
- attention shielding
- re-entry
- volatility control
- low-drift execution

The source framework is explicit that capacity training strengthens stability and flexibility needed to run the loop under load.

#### Y5.3 Operator training

Operator training teaches the primitive move language itself. This is the Trident-G operator algebra. It should remain common across the scaled family, even when different programmes emphasise different subsets.

#### Y5.4 Reasoning-style drills

Reasoning-style drills train the reasoning calculus that runs within the language of thought and action. These drills should vary in emphasis by programme, but the underlying reasoning repertoire remains shared.

#### Y5.5 Syntax training and syntax swaps

Scaled programmes should also explicitly train and test across **surface syntax**.

The same operator or mission chain should, where possible, be rendered across multiple syntaxes:

- **verbal syntax**: written argument, textual rule, verbal decision case
- **visuospatial syntax**: diagram, graph, matrix, spatial relation map
- **numerical syntax**: quantities, ratios, weighted trade-offs, probabilistic or tabular form

This is useful because a learner who succeeds only in one syntax may still be tied to surface familiarity. A learner who succeeds across syntaxes is showing stronger evidence that the deeper invariant has been abstracted and can be re-instantiated across forms. That is exactly the kind of selection pressure the far-transfer protocol is meant to impose.

#### Y5.6 Mission bootcamp

Mission bootcamp is the integrative layer where operators and reasoning styles are recombined inside real mission frames. This is where the learner runs full loops for understanding, argumentation, decision, strategic action, or selected control missions. It is also where semantic wrappers become more domain-specific.

#### Y5.7 Portability and banking

Every scaled programme should preserve the same endpoint logic:

- probe first
- validate under variation
- run syntax swaps
- run broader wrapper swaps
- run boundary or trap checks
- confirm cue-fired deployment
- compile only what survives into Gc as a banked script

The banked unit remains:

**cue / trigger → steps or operators → check → boundaries or traps → stop rule**

A useful default staircase for training is:

1. train an operator or mission chain in one syntax  
2. re-test it in another syntax  
3. re-test it in another wrapper or applied context  
4. test cue-fired deployment in real use

This staircase fits the source logic very well. Syntax swaps test whether the gain survives change in surface code. Broader wrapper swaps test whether it survives change in scenario family or applied context. Cue-fired deployment then tests whether the operator actually travels into real use.

### Y6. Core app, mission plugins, and breakout apps

The cleanest implementation is:

**one shared core engine**  
with  
**multiple mission-family plugins or breakout products**

#### Y6.1 Core engine

The core engine should contain the layers that remain common:

- zone and corridor gating
- capacity training
- operator training
- reasoning-style drills
- syntax library and syntax-swap testing
- progress logging
- portability checks
- script bank
- shared profile and history

This is the part that should remain structurally common across the product family.

#### Y6.2 Mission plugins

Mission plugins should define:

- which mission family is foregrounded
- which operator bundles are emphasised
- which reasoning styles are drilled most often
- which syntaxes are foregrounded most often
- which semantic wrappers are used
- which subjective coaching cues are foregrounded
- which proof signals count most

This is the cleanest way to scale because the plugin changes the **foreground mission logic**, not the core engine.

#### Y6.3 Breakout apps

Some layers can also be sold as narrower standalone products. The cleanest examples are:

**Zone Coach**  
viability gate, recovery routing, dose control

**Capacity Gym**  
working memory, attention stability, interference control, updating, re-entry

**Logic / Inference Gym**  
reasoning-style training in deductive, abductive, analogical, Bayesian, and value-of-information work

**Operator / G-Syntax Gym**  
training in the primitive operator language itself, with syntax variation and transfer checks across verbal, visuospatial, and numerical forms where applicable

**Mission Bootcamp**  
integrative mission training across frames

These are valid breakout products because each corresponds to a real architectural layer. But none alone is a full substitute for the integrated far-transfer stack.

### Y7. Different downstream emphases by programme

Once the shared engine is fixed, different programmes can legitimately emphasise different downstream targets.

#### Y7.1 IQ / Performance emphasis

This family will typically foreground more:

- deductive reasoning
- abductive reasoning
- Bayesian or statistical updating
- value-of-information reasoning
- analogy for transfer, reframing, and cross-domain problem solving

Typical operator emphasis is likely to include more use of:

- Y4 Map
- Y7 Analogise
- Y10 Re-represent
- Y12 Evaluate
- Y13 Discriminate / Probe
- Y15 Satisfice / Commit
- Y16 Level control

Capacity emphasis often falls on:

- relational holding
- mapping fluency
- updating
- switching under demand
- wrapper-robust reasoning under load

Typical syntax emphasis may include:

- verbal argument and explanation
- visuospatial mapping, graph and matrix reasoning
- numerical trade-off, probability, and weighting forms where relevant

#### Y7.2 Resilience / Self-regulation emphasis

This family will typically foreground more:

- evaluative checking
- reframing and re-representation
- discriminating genuine threat from noise
- meta-awareness of triggers and scripts
- boundary and stop-rule logic around rumination, overload, and misapplied control

Typical operator emphasis is likely to include more use of:

- Y1 Orient
- Y2 Recover
- Y3 Externalise / Escalate
- Y10 Re-represent
- Y12 Evaluate
- Y13 Discriminate / Probe
- Y15 Satisfice / Commit
- Y16 Level control

Capacity emphasis often falls on:

- re-entry
- attention shielding
- cadence
- volatility control
- maintaining viable in-band operation under distraction, fatigue, or pressure

Typical syntax emphasis may include:

- verbal self-instruction, reframing and cue rules
- visuospatial state maps, distance diagrams, pacing boards, or salience layouts
- numerical pacing, timing, threshold, or dose-control formats where relevant

This is not a different logic universe. It is the same language of thought and action under a different selection pressure.

### Y8. Domain packs and bespoke programmes

Beyond the two primary mission families, Trident-G should scale by adding **domain packs** or **bespoke programme packs**.

A pack should not change the kernel. It should change the syntax library, semantic wrapper library, operator emphasis, capacity emphasis, proof targets, and applied telemetry.

#### Y8.1 Pack definition

A clean pack is defined by eight things:

1. target population  
2. foreground mission family  
3. operator bundle emphasis  
4. reasoning-style emphasis  
5. capacity emphasis  
6. syntax emphasis  
7. semantic wrapper library  
8. telemetry and proof schema

This is the correct scaling unit for commercial and applied expansion.

#### Y8.2 Example packs

**Cognitive Longevity pack**  
A calm, older-friendly, plain-language programme that foregrounds a blend of Learn, Understand, Choose, Plan & Do, Recover / re-enter, Regulate cadence, and Shield attention. It should focus on healthy cognitive habits, everyday reliability, recovery, and applied reasoning rather than inflated claims. It should be framed as supporting healthy habits, not medical advice; consult your GP if concerned.

Typical syntax emphasis:
- verbal everyday reasoning prompts
- visuospatial calendars, maps, and routines
- simple numerical weighting, scheduling, or dose formats where useful

**Corporate Performance pack**  
A programme foregrounding Understand, Argue, Choose, Plan & Do, and Learn, with stronger emphasis on decision quality, argument audit, prioritisation, execution reliability, re-entry under interruption, and wrapper-robust strategic action.

Typical syntax emphasis:
- verbal briefs, claims and decisions
- visuospatial workflow maps, matrices, and dashboards
- numerical trade-off tables, prioritisation grids, and uncertainty formats

**Corporate Resilience pack**  
A programme foregrounding Shield attention, Regulate cadence, Prioritise missions, Create distance, Calibrate mode, Recover / re-enter, and Externalise / escalate, with telemetry focused on overload episodes, re-entry time, drift reduction, interruption recovery, and pacing adherence.

Typical syntax emphasis:
- verbal cueing and recovery scripts
- visuospatial workload boards and interruption maps
- numerical pacing thresholds, timing rules, and overload boundaries

These are not separate theories. They are different mission, syntax, and telemetry packs built on the same engine.

### Y9. Telemetry and proof at scale

Scaling should never collapse into content variety alone. The proof layer must scale with the programme.

#### Y9.1 Universal telemetry

Some telemetry belongs to the shared kernel and should remain common across packs:

- in-band versus out-of-band state
- drift
- error bursts
- re-entry speed
- syntax-swap pass rate
- broader swap cost
- wrapper robustness
- mission completion quality
- cue-fired deployment
- banking status

These indicators belong to kernel performance and far-transfer discipline.

#### Y9.2 Pack-specific telemetry

Other telemetry should vary by pack.

For example:

- cognitive longevity packs may care more about consistency, applied everyday cognition, recovery readiness, and low-drift engagement
- corporate performance packs may care more about decision quality, prioritisation, execution reliability, interruption recovery, and rework avoidance
- corporate resilience packs may care more about overload episodes, cadence adherence, drift reduction, re-entry time, and adherence to recovery rules

The general rule is:

**same kernel telemetry, different applied proof schema**

#### Y9.3 Success thresholds

What counts as useful improvement can also vary by programme, but the basic far-transfer rule does not. If a pack wants to claim genuine transfer, it still needs portability checks, real cue-fired deployment, and conservative banking. Syntax robustness may count as an earlier or nearer portability threshold, but it is not the whole of far transfer by itself.

### Y10. Subjectivity and coaching emphasis across scaled programmes

Because Trident-G treats subjectivity as the lived interface of the loop, scaling also changes which subjective cues are foregrounded.

A performance-focused programme may foreground:

- clarity
- confidence calibration
- decision pressure
- curiosity
- strategic uncertainty
- commitment versus hovering

A resilience-focused programme may foreground:

- overload
- felt urgency
- distraction capture
- aversion
- rumination pull
- fatigue
- recovery readiness
- felt distance from the task

This does not create a separate architecture. It changes how the programme reads and coaches the subjective interface in relation to the semantic layer and the feedback board.

### Y11. Scaling path: from standalone tools to integrated system

The strongest product path is:

#### Y11.1 Standalone-first

Launch narrow but coherent standalones built around real architectural layers, such as Zone Coach, Capacity Gym, Logic / Inference Gym, Operator / G-Syntax Gym, and Mission Bootcamp.

#### Y11.2 Shared engine behind the scenes

Keep one shared backend and one shared engine logic so that user history, script banking, telemetry, mission packs, syntax packs, and later integration remain coherent.

#### Y11.3 Integrated flagship later

The fully integrated flagship should combine:

- viability gating
- capacity training
- language-of-thought training
- syntax variation and syntax-swap testing
- mission bootcamps
- portability testing
- banked scripts
- cross-pack transfer tracking

In other words, the standalone tools are specialised entry points. The integrated app is the full far-transfer system.

### Y12. Canonical scaling rule

The cleanest one-line summary is:

**Do not change the kernel. Change the foreground mission family, operator emphasis, reasoning emphasis, capacity emphasis, syntax and semantic wrappers, telemetry, and user-facing framing.**

### Y13. Canonical summary

Trident-G training programmes should scale as a family of mission-, syntax-, and domain-specific products built on one shared architecture. Across the whole family, the two meta-missions of Viability and Expansion remain fixed. The G-Loop kernel remains fixed. The language of thought and action, composed of operator algebra and mission grammar, remains fixed. The reasoning calculus remains fixed. The cognitive runtime and Ψ-allostatic viability logic remain fixed. What changes across products is the applied mission family foregrounded, the operator bundles most emphasised, the reasoning styles most drilled, the capacity targets most trained, the syntax emphasis, the semantic wrapper library, the subjective coaching emphasis, and the telemetry or proof schema. This allows Trident-G to scale into breakout apps, mission plugins, cognitive longevity programmes, corporate performance packs, corporate resilience packs, and later integrated flagships without fragmenting into separate theories. It preserves one common engine while allowing multiple sellable training programmes and domain-specific evidence layers.
