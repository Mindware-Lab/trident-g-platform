# Trident-G mission framework

## 1. Purpose

The mission framework specifies the user-facing purpose layer of Trident-G. It answers the question:

**What is the loop being run for right now?**

This framework sits below the two highest-level meta-missions of **Viability** and **Expansion**, and above the formal layer of operators, tactics, strategies, mission grammar, and reasoning calculus. Its role is not to replace the G-Loop, but to tell the G-Loop what counts as success in the present run.

A mission frame therefore defines what counts as:

- a good map
- a good strategy
- a good tactic
- a good test
- a good update
- a good gist
- a good stopping point

The same shared kernel may be used in every frame, but the success criterion changes by frame.

## 2. Core distinction

The mission framework is best understood as having **two applied mission families** and **one cross-cutting acquisition frame**.

### A. Mission frames — IQ / performance purposes

These are the main user-facing performance purposes for which the loop may be run:

- **Understand**
- **Argue**
- **Choose**
- **Plan & Do**

These are the core applied mission frames because they define distinct kinds of successful action or cognition.

### B. Control missions — resilience / self-regulation purposes

These are the user-facing purposes concerned with keeping the loop viable, clean, and repeatable under real conditions:

- **Shield attention**
- **Regulate cadence**
- **Prioritise missions**
- **Create distance**
- **Calibrate mode**
- **Arbitrate improvisation vs script**
- **Recover / re-enter**
- **Externalise / escalate**

These do not define the main truth- or action-goal of the run. They define what protects clean execution.

### C. Learn — cross-cutting acquisition frame

**Learn** is best treated not as a fifth parallel mission frame, but as a **cross-cutting acquisition frame** that can operate within any of the core mission frames.

Its purpose is to:

- acquire a new capability
- stabilise a fragile capability
- improve reliability under variation
- transfer a capability across wrappers, syntaxes, or contexts
- prepare something for later banking into Gc

So rather than sitting beside Understand, Argue, Choose, and Plan & Do as a fully separate type, Learn is better understood as a way of running those frames when the goal is acquisition, stabilisation, or transfer.

For example:

- **Learn-to-understand**
- **Learn-to-argue**
- **Learn-to-choose**
- **Learn-to-plan and do**

This keeps the architecture cleaner. The main mission frames remain the core applied purposes. Learn modifies how they are being run.

## 3. The four core mission frames

### 3.1 Understand | Comprehension

**Purpose:** build a usable model of what is going on.

A successful Understand run produces a map that is clearer, more predictive, and more useful for the next move. The aim is not merely to accumulate detail, but to arrive at a representation that explains structure well enough to support good inference or action.

**Primary success criterion:** a clearer map that predicts, explains, and supports a safe, informative next move.

Typical questions in this frame include:

- What is happening here?
- What is the real structure of the problem?
- What constrains what?
- What matters most?
- What am I missing?

### 3.2 Argue | Argumentation

**Purpose:** produce, test, refine, or audit a claim.

A successful Argue run makes the structure of support explicit. It clarifies warrants, assumptions, boundaries, objections, alternatives, and the strength of the claim under pressure. The aim is not verbal display, but disciplined claim-testing.

**Primary success criterion:** explicit claim-support discipline with validity checks, counter-consideration, and boundaries.

Typical questions in this frame include:

- What exactly is being claimed?
- What supports it?
- What weakens it?
- What follows if it is true?
- What counterexample or contradiction matters here?

### 3.3 Choose | Decision

**Purpose:** select an action under uncertainty and trade-offs.

A successful Choose run does not eliminate all uncertainty. It clarifies the available options, trade-offs, expected consequences, information gaps, and stopping point for commitment. The aim is justified selection under imperfect knowledge.

**Primary success criterion:** a justified choice with explicit trade-offs, acknowledged uncertainty, and a clear reason for selection.

Typical questions in this frame include:

- What are the live options?
- What matters most in selecting between them?
- What uncertainty remains?
- What probe would most improve the choice?
- Is there enough signal to commit?

### 3.4 Plan & Do | Strategic action

**Purpose:** turn a map or choice into executable action, then adapt under feedback.

A successful Plan & Do run produces not only a plan, but an executable sequence with monitoring, correction, and follow-through. The aim is not static planning but guided action under reality.

**Primary success criterion:** an executable sequence with a measurable signal, monitoring, and real-world follow-through.

Typical questions in this frame include:

- What is the next step?
- What sequence will move this forward?
- What do I monitor?
- What counts as progress?
- What needs updating if reality pushes back?

## 4. Learn as a cross-cutting acquisition frame

Learn is best understood as a **cross-cutting wrapper** around the four core mission frames. It changes the emphasis of the run from immediate performance alone to acquisition, stabilisation, and transfer.

### 4.1 Purpose of Learn

The purpose of Learn is to acquire, stabilise, and transfer a new capability, method, or schema.

It does this by asking not only:

- Can I do this now?

but also:

- Can I do it again?
- Can I explain what the cue is?
- Can I run it in a new wrapper?
- Can I preserve the invariant when the surface changes?
- Is this now stable enough to bank?

### 4.2 Primary success criterion

**Primary success criterion:** the learner can instantiate the skill or schema in a new wrapper, explain the cue, and show early portability.

### 4.3 Why Learn is cross-cutting

Learn should be treated as cross-cutting because acquisition can occur inside any of the main mission frames.

Examples:

- in **Understand**, the person may be learning how to build a better map
- in **Argue**, the person may be learning how to evaluate claims or generate counterexamples
- in **Choose**, the person may be learning a decision routine or information-gain tactic
- in **Plan & Do**, the person may be learning a script for execution and adaptation under feedback

In each case, the kernel is still being run for understanding, argument, choice, or action. Learn changes the orientation of the run from immediate success alone to **capability acquisition plus future portability**.

### 4.4 Relation to Install / Bank

Learn should also be distinguished from **Install / Bank**.

- **Learn** is the acquisition-oriented way of running a mission.
- **Install / Bank** is the cross-frame conversion mode by which something proven useful becomes reusable competence in Gc.

So Learn may prepare something for banking, but it is not identical with banking.

## 5. Control missions

Control missions are not secondary in the sense of being unimportant. They are secondary only in the sense that they do not define the main epistemic or action-purpose of the run. Their job is to protect clean execution.

### 5.1 Shield attention

Protect the task-set from distraction, salience hijack, interruption, and irrelevant pulls.

### 5.2 Regulate cadence

Match pace to viable state and task demand so the loop neither rushes nor stalls.

### 5.3 Prioritise missions

Select which mission deserves effort now and suppress lower-value competition.

### 5.4 Create distance

Reduce over-identification, panic, or salience capture so the map can be seen more clearly.

### 5.5 Calibrate mode

Adjust between widening and tightening, exploration and control, improvisation and script.

### 5.6 Arbitrate improvisation vs script

Decide when to trust stored structure and when novelty requires live restructuring.

### 5.7 Recover / re-enter

Restore workable corridor occupancy and resume clean execution after disruption, overload, or drift.

### 5.8 Externalise / escalate

Offload cognition into scaffolds or support systems when clean in-head continuation is no longer viable or safe.

## 6. Relation between mission frames, Learn, and control missions

The cleanest relationship is:

- **Mission frames** define the main purpose of the run.
- **Learn** defines whether that run is being carried out in acquisition mode.
- **Control missions** define what keeps the run viable and repeatable.

So the framework should be read like this:

- **Understand** may be run normally or in **Learn** mode.
- **Choose** may be run normally or in **Learn** mode.
- **Plan & Do** may be run normally or in **Learn** mode.
- any of these may require one or more **control missions** to stay viable.

This produces a cleaner architecture than treating Learn as simply one more item in a flat list.

## 7. Mission framework within the wider architecture

The mission framework sits within the wider Trident-G architecture as follows:

- **Meta-missions** specify what the whole system is trying to become.
- **Mission frames** specify what the present run is for.
- **Learn** specifies whether the run is acquisition-oriented.
- **Control missions** specify what protects viable execution.
- **Meta-dials** shape how well the system runs over time.
- **Feedback signals** indicate what kind of update is needed now.
- **The formal layer** supplies the moves, tactics, strategies, and sequencing rules by which the mission is executed.

## 8. Canonical summary

The Trident-G mission framework is the applied purpose layer of the architecture. Its core mission frames are **Understand, Argue, Choose, and Plan & Do**, which define the main user-facing purposes for which the G-Loop may be run. Alongside these sits a family of **control missions**, which protect viability, stability, and repeatability under real conditions. **Learn** is best treated as a cross-cutting acquisition frame rather than as a fifth parallel mission frame: it can wrap any of the core mission frames when the current goal is to acquire, stabilise, or transfer a capability. In this way, the framework preserves a clean distinction between purpose, acquisition, and viability control while allowing all three to interact within one shared G-Loop kernel.

## 9. General rule

**Mission frames define purpose. Learn defines acquisition mode. Control missions protect viability.**
