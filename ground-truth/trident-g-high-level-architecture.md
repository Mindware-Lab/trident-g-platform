# Appendix XX. High-level architecture of Trident-G

## X0. Purpose

This appendix sets out the high-level architecture of Trident-G in its most general form. Its aim is to clarify how the framework distinguishes:

- what the loop is for
- what the loop is about
- how the loop thinks and acts
- how the loop is shaped by slower meta-level governance
- how the loop is shaped by subjective valuation
- under what viability geometry the loop remains clean and recoverable
- what runtime substrate makes the whole system executable

The result is a layered but integrated view of Trident-G as a domain-general intelligence architecture organised around one shared G-Loop kernel. The same kernel can be run across different mission frames, domains, data sources, syntaxes, wrappers, and action surfaces while preserving a common control-and-inference logic.

A further clarification is important. The G-Loop does not merely support viable inference and action. It also progressively extracts gist, selects deeper invariants, and compiles what survives into reusable Gc. The high-level architecture should therefore be read not only as an architecture for reasoning, but also as an architecture for selective compression, portability testing, and crystallisation.

## X1. The meta-level comes first

Above the applied layer, Trident-G includes a clearer meta-level architecture. At the highest level sit two meta-missions, and beneath them sits a meta-dial layer. This distinction matters because the framework should not collapse purpose and means into one flat list. The meta-missions specify what the whole system is trying to become. The meta-dials specify the slower trainable governance qualities by which it gets there.

### X1.1 Meta-missions

The two highest-level meta-missions are:

**Viability**  
Maintain adaptive operation within the defended Ψ corridor so learning, inference, and action remain clean and recoverable.

**Expansion**  
Enlarge viable dynamic range and portable operator repertoire through exploration, exploitation, gist extraction, validation, and crystallisation into reusable Gc.

These meta-missions should be treated not only as architectural statements, but also as trainable global orientations. Viability becomes the cultivated preference for clean, recoverable in-band functioning over overheat, flatness, panic-grind, or unstable forcing. Expansion becomes the cultivated preference for enlarging live reasoning range and banked portable competence rather than merely securing local wrapper-bound success.

### X1.2 Meta-dials

Beneath the meta-missions sits the meta-dial layer. These are not in-session missions. They are slower shapers of cognitive geometry that alter controllability, learnability, corridor width, ecological support, compression quality, proof discipline, scale fit, and overall noise over longer timescales. The current canonical meta-dials are:

- meta-efficacy
- coherence
- compression depth
- complexity governance
- subjective calibration
- proof discipline
- progress–quality governance
- exploration–exploitation governance
- multi-scale governance
- ecology support

These are distributed over operators and visible in behavioural, subjective, and telemetry patterns across repeated loops. They shape how well the kernel is run across time and across contexts.

## X2. Mission frames come first at the applied level

At the highest applied level, Trident-G begins with mission frames. These answer the question:

**What is the loop being run for right now?**

Mission frames define the purpose of the current run. They determine what counts as a good map, a good strategy, a good tactic, a good test, a good update, a good gist, and a good stopping point for the current run. The same underlying operator algebra and mission grammar may be used in every frame, but the success criterion changes by frame.

The core mission frames are:

### X2.1 Understand | Comprehension

**Purpose:** build a usable model of what is going on.  
**Primary success criterion:** a clearer map that predicts, explains, and supports a safe, informative next move.

### X2.2 Argue | Argumentation

**Purpose:** produce, test, refine, or audit a claim.  
**Primary success criterion:** explicit claim-support discipline with validity checks, counter-consideration, and boundaries.

### X2.3 Choose | Decision

**Purpose:** select an action under uncertainty and trade-offs.  
**Primary success criterion:** a justified choice with explicit trade-offs, acknowledged uncertainty, and a clear reason for selection.

### X2.4 Plan & Do | Strategic action

**Purpose:** turn a map or choice into executable action, then adapt under feedback.  
**Primary success criterion:** an executable sequence with a measurable signal, monitoring, and real-world follow-through.

### X2.5 Learn | Acquisition frame

**Purpose:** acquire, stabilise, and transfer a new capability, method, or schema.  
**Primary success criterion:** the learner can instantiate the skill or schema in a new wrapper, explain the cue, and show early portability.

Architecturally, Learn is best treated as a user-facing acquisition frame, while Install / Bank remains a cross-frame conversion mode that can follow any successful mission once something proves reusable. Mission frames therefore define the top-level applied purpose of the run. They do not replace the kernel. They tell the kernel what counts as success here, including what kind of structure is worth extracting and stabilising for future use.

## X3. The wider Trident-G distinction already present in the framework

Mission frames sit inside a wider architecture already present in Trident-G. Four surrounding distinctions remain essential.

### X3.1 Control missions

Control missions keep the loop viable, clean, and repeatable under distraction, pressure, fatigue, overheat, flatness, and fluctuating salience. Their target is not truth or performance directly, but clean loop execution inside the defended Ψ-band.

### X3.2 Meta-dials

Meta-dials are slower shapers of cognitive geometry. They alter controllability, learnability, corridor width, ecological support, proof discipline, compression depth, scale fit, and noise over longer timescales. They are not extra mission frames and not extra primitive operators. They are trainable governance qualities that shape how well the loop runs.

### X3.3 Feedback signals

Feedback signals are fast diagnostics. These include load, drift, mismatch, unexpected mismatch, salience, complexity, and conflict. They do not define the goal. They tell the loop whether to simplify, probe, recover, update, or restructure.

### X3.4 G-Loop kernel

The G-Loop itself remains the core intelligence kernel: the smallest repeatable cycle that keeps cognition viable inside the defended Ψ corridor while turning successful inference into reusable competence. It runs by mapping, testing, committing, updating, compressing, and banking under mission criteria and signals.

The present appendix preserves these distinctions and adds further clarity to the formal, semantic, subjective, allostatic, and runtime sides of the architecture.

## X4. Language of thought and action

Trident-G can be described as having a higher-level language of thought and action.

**Language of thought and action = operator algebra + mission grammar**

This language is the compositional system by which intelligent moves are built and run. It is the concrete mindware implementation layer of the kernel, but it is not the whole kernel by itself.

### X4.1 Operator algebra

The operator algebra is the set of primitive mindware moves by which the G-Loop sets rigour, builds and transforms structure, tests and discriminates, commits action, protects viability, and validates portability. These operators are not the raw processing substrate itself. They are rule-like moves that act on structured representational objects such as maps, chunks, schemas, claims, hypotheses, action candidates, and external artefacts. Working memory, relational binding capacity, and attention keep these objects active, structured, and manipulable enough for the operator layer to act on them. In Trident-G, the canonical operator set is the Y1–Y16 family. These are compositional mindware moves that support both performance missions and resilience missions.

### X4.2 Mission grammar

Mission grammar is the set of frame-specific well-formedness rules that governs how operators should be sequenced within a given mission frame. It specifies which sequences of moves are appropriate, diagnostically useful, and success-relevant in a given context. The same operator algebra can therefore be used across frames, but the grammar differs because what counts as a good map, a good test, a good update, a good gist, and a good stop differs between understanding, argumentation, choice, and execution. The operator algebra supplies the vocabulary of intelligent moves. Mission grammar supplies the compositional rules for assembling them into a good run.

### X4.3 Tactics and strategies

Within this wider architecture, two further distinctions are now useful.

**Tactics** are local, reusable compositions of operators. A tactic is a structured way of making progress at a particular point in a problem space. It is more specific than a broad strategy but more complex than a single primitive operator.

**Strategies** are broader problem-space approaches that select and sequence tactics in service of the current mission frame. A strategy therefore sits above tactics and below mission frames. It does not define the top-level purpose of the loop, but the general way the loop will try to succeed within that purpose.

This means the formal hierarchy can be stated cleanly:

- mission frame = what the run is for
- strategy = the broader approach within that run
- tactic = the local reusable problem-space move
- operator = the primitive formal move-language

This preserves the compact operator set while allowing richer problem-solving structure to be named without inflating the primitive layer.

## X5. Reasoning calculus

Separate from the language of thought and action, though operating within it, is the reasoning calculus.

**Reasoning calculus = inferential transformations run within that language**

The framework distinguishes the operators from the cross-cutting reasoning styles used during operator execution. Deductive, inductive, abductive, analogical, Bayesian or statistical, and value-of-information reasoning are not separate top-level operators. They are inference styles layered onto operator runs. The operator says what move is being made. The reasoning style says how inference is being carried out during that move.

This yields a clean formal architecture:

- operator algebra = what moves exist
- mission grammar = what sequences are well-formed for this purpose
- reasoning calculus = what inferential transformations are executed within those moves and sequences
- tactics = local reusable operator compositions
- strategies = broader problem-space approaches that select and sequence tactics

Reasoning is not only in the service of finding the next correct move. It is also in the service of extracting the most useful compression of the current structure: the gist, invariant, or portable principle that can later be tested and banked.

### X5.1 Strategy families in the reasoning calculus

The updated problem-solving material also makes it useful to name broader strategy families that may organise tactic choice inside the loop. These are not new primitives. They are recurring approaches to structured problem solving.

**Deductive strategies**  
Necessary consequence, exclusion, contradiction handling, constraint propagation, counterexample testing.

**Exploratory model-space strategies**  
Abduction, counterfactual simulation, analogical reasoning, causal structure search.

**Probe-selection strategies**  
Value-of-information, expected information gain, discriminating test design.

**Search / route strategies**  
Means–ends reasoning, bidirectional search, operator sequencing.

**Update strategies**  
Bayesian or statistical updating, confidence-calibrated updating.

**Structural-map strategies**  
Graph-theoretic reasoning, connectivity or reachability reasoning.

These strategy families help describe how the loop searches a problem space without changing the architecture of the kernel.

### X5.2 Canonical tactics

The updated problem-solving patterns are best treated as canonical tactics rather than as new operators.

**Constraint-induced narrowing**  
Use evaluation and probing to eliminate impossibilities and tighten the current space.

**Strategic perturbation**  
Use a small, informative test to reveal hidden structure or separate rival explanations.

**Bidirectional constraint squeezing**  
Build the space from both ends, hold the goal state explicitly, and shrink the route space until a workable bridge appears.

**Working-hypothesis stabilisation**  
Commit defeasibly to the best current hypothesis so that it can be tested, updated, or abandoned cleanly.

**Forced-move commitment**  
When the space is sufficiently constrained, commit to the only viable next move rather than hovering.

**Global connectivity guarding**  
Preserve the viability of the whole structure by checking whether crucial nodes, paths, or bottlenecks remain intact.

**Confidence-calibrated updating**  
Update belief or policy in proportion to evidence quality and reliability rather than by mere emotional force or local fluency.

These tactics are reusable local patterns of problem-space work executed through the primitive operator language.

## X6. Semantic layer

The formal language does not operate over empty symbols. Trident-G therefore includes an explicit semantic layer.

**Semantic layer**

- ontology
- situation map
- affordance surface

The formal layer says how the system can reason. The semantic layer says what the reasoning is about.

### X6.1 Ontology

The ontology is the structured inventory of what exists in the relevant bounded world. It specifies the deep semantic structure of the domain: entities, states, relations, constraints, mechanisms, signals, values, risks, and affordances.

### X6.2 Situation map

The situation map is the current usable representation of the present case. In Trident-G terms, a good map is both a predictive structure and a value landscape: what tends to lead to what, what is constrained, what options exist, and what matters under the present stakes. With the newer problem-space clarification, it may also include current state, target state, available routes, bottlenecks, candidate tactics, and local boundaries or traps. A good map is not the largest possible model, but the simplest one that is accurate enough to guide the next move. When load or drift is high, simplification becomes more urgent. When mismatch is high, more structure may briefly be needed to locate what is wrong.

Building and maintaining a good situation map depends not only on keeping elements active in working memory, but on forming and maintaining temporary, arbitrary, novel role–filler bindings that allow the system to construct, compare, and revise new structure online rather than merely retrieving fixed associations.

### X6.3 Affordance surface

The affordance surface is the live action layer exposed by the current map: what can be probed, changed, selected, delegated, monitored, deferred, or executed now. In the updated formulation, this also includes what tactics are locally available and what strategy transitions are currently possible.

### X6.4 Syntax layer

The semantic layer specifies what the current model is about. The syntax layer specifies how that model is rendered at the surface. In Trident-G, the same deeper structure may be expressed in multiple syntaxes, including verbal, visuospatial, and numerical form, where applicable. Syntax should therefore be treated as a surface coding layer rather than as the deep structure itself. Syntax swaps are a controlled subtype of wrapper swap and are used to test whether deeper semantic structure and operator demand survive changes in surface code.

## X7. Subjectivity as the lived interface of the loop

Trident-G treats subjectivity as architecturally central rather than as a mere by-product of reasoning. Feelings, motivations, emotions, urges, and felt significance are the lived interface through which the system encounters both the affordance surface and the underlying situation map. Subjectivity registers not only what is present in the environment or model, but what is good or bad for the organism, what is costly or urgent, what is attractive or threatening, and what level of effort or caution is warranted now.

### X7.1 Subjectivity as the phenomenal face of valuation

A good map is not only predictive structure but also a value landscape. Subjectivity is how that value landscape is lived in conscious and instinctive control: pressure, confidence, dread, curiosity, relief, attraction, aversion, coherence, confusion, or felt click. These experiences do not replace formal inference, but they shape salience, attention allocation, action competition, and stop rules.

### X7.2 Subjectivity as the interface to affordances

The affordance surface is not encountered as a flat menu of options. It is encountered through felt pull and push: approach, avoidance, urgency, dread, confidence, curiosity, frustration, or desire. In that sense, subjectivity is one of the ways the organism registers what matters, what is possible, and what should be acted on now.

### X7.3 Subjectivity as signal-rich but fallible

Subjectivity should not be treated as either pure truth or pure noise. It is signal-rich but fallible. Some feelings disclose real significance. Others reflect over-bracing, threat bias, fatigue, flatness, stale priors, or distorted appraisal. Subjective experience often carries information about several distinguishable costs at once: the cost of revising the current model, the cost of carrying the current map, and the total felt burden of running the loop under present state and stakes. These should not be confused, but subjectivity is one of the places where they are first encountered.

### X7.4 Subjectivity in training and coaching

In training and coaching, affective pressure should not be treated as irrelevant noise. Emotion can raise load, alter salience, bias action competition, destabilise the task-set, or distort the current map. Conversely, distancing, reappraisal, pacing, and recovery can restore viable inference and cleaner credit assignment. Subjectivity is also relevant to compression success. Relief, coherence, clarity, or excitement may be useful candidate markers that something important has been grasped, but pleasurable compression is not proof of portability. A rewarding click is a candidate signal of emerging gist, not by itself evidence of far transfer.

## X8. Ψ-allostatic geometry

Trident-G is not only a symbolic or inferential architecture. It is also a state-sensitive intelligence architecture. For that reason, the framework includes an explicit Ψ-allostatic layer.

**Allostatic layer = Ψ-geometry / viability regime**

This is the defended state-space geometry within which inference, learning, and action remain clean and recoverable. The framework states that the first question in any loop is whether the system is in a workable state to run a clean loop. Above the corridor lie overheat, thrash, and panic-grind. Below it lie flatness, boredom-drift, or protective disengagement.

Within this allostatic geometry:

- load tracks current realised burden
- drift tracks task-set decay
- mismatch tracks plan–reality or model–reality gap
- unexpected mismatch gates deeper restructuring
- salience sets rigour budget
- complexity tracks map cost
- conflict acts as a cue for sharper evaluation or discrimination

The allostatic layer is therefore not another mission family. It is the viability geometry within which the other layers operate. The complexity named here is the burden of carrying the current map, not the cost of belief revision as such. The latter is better treated as inferential updating cost sitting behind mismatch-driven revision.

### X8.1 Subjectivity and allostasis

Subjectivity should be treated as the lived surface of allostatic control. Feelings are part of how the organism tracks bodily significance, cost, and action-readiness within a defended viability regime. This also helps explain why enjoyable challenge is an important phenomenological marker. A state of enjoyable challenge arises when complexity is high enough to engage the system, but still low enough for updating, compression, and action to remain viable. In practice, this is close to an in-band state where salience is meaningful, effort is acceptable, and the map does not collapse under its own weight.

## X9. Cognitive runtime

Below the formal, semantic, subjective, allostatic, and meta-level layers sits the cognitive runtime.

### X9.1 Runtime, broadly

The runtime is the whole executable substrate:

- working memory
- relational binding capacity
- attention
- control stability
- interference resistance
- re-entry quality
- viable computation under load

The framework already distinguishes the runtime from the mindware layer. Working memory, relational binding capacity, attention protection, interference control, re-entry quality, and relational holding power belong primarily to the capacity substrate. The operators themselves are not the raw processing. They are rule-like moves that act on what the runtime keeps active and manipulable.

The live centre depends especially on five practical capacities: relational fluid intelligence, working memory, relational binding capacity, attention management, and relational inference or updating capacity. Together these make the inference algebra runnable under load and make Gf discoveries bankable as Gc when they survive validation.

### X9.2 Fast relational runtime: SR-like predictive bridge

Within the broader runtime, there can also be a fast predictive relational layer. This can be understood as an SR-like predictive relational bridge that carries local structure forward, supports multi-step expectation, tracks likely next states and relations, and provides a live map that operators, tactics, and strategies can act over. It is best treated as a computational middle layer within the runtime rather than as the whole runtime itself.

## X10. Integrated stack

Putting the pieces together, the high-level Trident-G stack can be described as follows:

- Meta-missions → Viability | Expansion
- Meta-dials → slower shapers of cognitive geometry and training quality
- Mission frames → Understand | Argue | Choose | Plan & Do | Learn
- Control missions → Shield attention | Regulate cadence | Prioritise missions | Create distance | Calibrate mode | Arbitrate improvisation vs script | Recover / re-enter | Externalise / escalate
- Feedback signals → load | drift | mismatch | unexpected mismatch | salience | complexity | conflict
- Language of thought and action = operator algebra + mission grammar
- Tactics → local reusable operator compositions
- Strategies → broader problem-space approaches that select and sequence tactics
- Reasoning calculus → inferential transformations run within that language
- Semantic layer → ontology → situation map → affordance surface
- Syntax layer → verbal → visuospatial → numerical
- Subjective interface → feelings → motivations → emotions → felt salience / significance → urges, aversions, confidence, pressure → effort, relief, coherence, confusion, click
- Allostatic layer → Ψ-geometry / viability regime
- Cognitive runtime → working memory → relational binding capacity → attention → control stability → interference resistance → re-entry quality → viable computation under load
- Fast relational runtime → SR-like predictive relational bridge

This stack should not be read as a set of isolated modules. The layers are nested and mutually dependent. Mission frames specify what success means. Meta-dials shape how well the loop can be run over time. The language of thought and action specifies what structured move sequences are available. Tactics specify reusable local composites. Strategies specify broader problem-space approaches. The reasoning calculus specifies how inference transforms maps, claims, options, commitments, and candidate gists within those sequences. The semantic layer specifies what the system is modelling. The subjective interface specifies how significance, cost, possibility, threat, effort, and compression success are lived. The allostatic layer specifies whether the current regime is viable. The runtime specifies what can actually be held, transformed, and executed.

## X11. Relation to the G-Loop kernel

The G-Loop remains the core kernel running across all these layers. It is still the smallest repeatable cycle that keeps cognition viable inside the defended Ψ corridor while converting successful inference into reusable competence. It still runs by mapping, testing, committing, updating, compressing, and banking under mission criteria and signals.

What this appendix adds is a clearer statement of what each layer contributes:

- the meta-missions tell the architecture what the whole system is trying to become
- the meta-dials tell the architecture which slower governance qualities shape performance across time
- the mission frames tell the loop what it is for right now
- the control missions tell the loop what protects viable execution
- the feedback signals tell the loop what kind of adjustment is needed now
- the language of thought and action tells the loop what formal moves and sequences it can use
- the tactics tell the loop what local reusable composites are available
- the strategies tell the loop what broader problem-space approach is being used
- the reasoning calculus tells the loop how inferential transformation proceeds within those moves
- the semantic layer tells the loop what bounded world it is modelling
- the subjective interface tells the loop how significance, effort, cost, and compression are lived
- the allostatic layer tells the loop whether the current operating regime is viable
- the runtime tells the loop what can actually be executed under current conditions

## X12. Canonical summary

Trident-G should be understood as a domain-general intelligence architecture organised across several interacting layers. Above the applied layer sit two highest-level meta-missions, Viability and Expansion, together with a slower meta-dial layer that shapes how well the system is run across time and across contexts. Mission frames define the purpose of the current run. Control missions protect viable execution. A language of thought and action, composed of an operator algebra and a mission grammar, defines the reusable mindware moves and their well-formed sequencing. Within that wider structure, tactics are local reusable compositions of operators, and strategies are broader problem-space approaches that select and sequence those tactics within a mission frame. A reasoning calculus specifies the inferential transformations carried out within that language. A semantic layer, consisting of ontology, situation map, affordance surface, and surface syntax, defines the bounded world the system is modelling and how that structure is rendered. A subjective interface, consisting of feelings, motivations, emotions, felt significance, and lived effort, is the valuation surface through which the organism encounters that world. A Ψ-allostatic layer defines the defended viability geometry within which inference, learning, and action remain clean and recoverable. A cognitive runtime, including working memory, relational binding capacity, attention, stability, interference resistance, re-entry, and viable bandwidth under load, makes the whole system executable, while a fast SR-like relational bridge provides a live predictive middle layer for structure-sensitive reasoning. The G-Loop runs across all of these layers as the shared kernel by which Trident-G integrates purpose, modelling, subjective valuation, inference, tactics, strategies, selective compression, state control, portability testing, and runtime execution into one architecture.

**General rule:** mission frames define purpose, strategies define broader approach, tactics define local reusable composites, and operators define primitive moves.
