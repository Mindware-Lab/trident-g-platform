# Appendix X. The G-Loop as the core intelligence kernel of Trident-G

## X0. Purpose

The G-Loop is the core intelligence kernel of Trident-G. It is the smallest repeatable cycle that both keeps cognition viable inside the defended Ψ corridor and converts successful inference into reusable know-how. It is therefore not merely a training loop, a motivational cycle, or a problem-solving checklist. It is a general control-and-learning kernel for intelligent action under constraint.

In Trident-G, the same kernel can be run for different purposes and in different substrates. At the human level, it describes how intelligent cognition stays viable, updates its model, compresses and extracts what matters, and crystallises reusable competence. At the applied level, it can organise training, decision workflows, research systems, business operations, or other human-plus-tool environments, because the kernel itself is defined in domain-general terms: map, test, commit, update, and bank under signals, constraints, and mission criteria.

A crucial clarification is that the loop is not only about producing a correct next move. It is also about extracting the gist of what is structurally important, selecting what is likely to travel beyond the current wrapper, and compiling that into reusable competence. In that sense, the G-Loop is both an adaptive inference loop and a selective compression loop.

## X1. Core definition

The G-Loop is the smallest repeatable cycle that:

- keeps the system inside, or restores it to, a workable range of viable operation, the Ψ-band, and
- turns good thinking plus real checks into reusable know-how.

In everyday terms, it is a think–check–bank loop. In theoretical terms, it is a nested adaptive inference cycle that allocates rigour, builds a usable model, tests that model against reality, extracts the gist or deeper invariant of what matters, commits to a next move, updates under feedback, and crystallises what survives into portable Gc.

The loop should therefore be understood as doing two things at once. It supports viable intelligent action in the present. It also compresses and compiles what survives into reusable structure for future use.

## X2. Why it is a kernel, not just a training loop

The G-Loop should be treated as the kernel because everything else in Trident-G depends on it.

Mission frames do not replace it. They specify what the loop is being run for. Control missions do not replace it. They keep it viable. Meta-dials do not replace it. They shape the longer-timescale geometry within which it runs. Feedback signals do not replace it. They diagnose what sort of update is needed now.

That is why the G-Loop can be treated as the common engine across very different applications. The purpose changes. The kernel does not. In one case it may be used for comprehension in the brain-training stack. In another it may be used to run a research workflow, a decision platform, a trading workflow, or a sovereign operations stack. What changes is the mission frame, the success signal, the data source, and the action surface. What stays constant is the control-and-inference logic.

This includes not only the logic of mapping, probing, commitment, and updating, but also the logic of selective compression: extracting what is merely local from what is structurally portable, and storing only the latter as competence.

## X3. Highest-level architecture

At the deepest level, Trident-G is organised as a shaft-and-tines structure.

The shaft is Gc: the banked library of validated scripts, chunks, concepts, and compressed invariants, or crystallised competence. The centre tine is Gf: live, effortful, flexible reasoning. The outer tines are the two dominant higher-engagement branches: entropy-widening exploration and mutual-information-tightening control.

The G-Loop is the operational cycle that coordinates these parts. It keeps live reasoning anchored to viable state, allows widening or tightening as needed, extracts the gist of what the current structure implies, and crystallises what survives into reusable competence.

Above this sit two highest-level meta-missions:

- **Viability/Criticality/Flow**: maintain adaptive operation within the defended Ψ corridor so learning, inference, and action remain clean and recoverable.
- **Expansion/G Augmentation**: enlarge viable dynamic range and portable operator repertoire through the coordination of exploration, exploitation, gist extraction, and crystallisation into Gc.

These two meta-missions explain what the kernel is for in the broadest sense. In training terms, they should also be treated as trainable global orientations: a cultivated preference for stable, recoverable in-band function and for enlarging live reasoning range and banked portable competence, rather than merely securing local wrapper-bound success.

## X4. The four surrounding layers

To describe the G-Loop cleanly, four surrounding layers should be kept distinct.

**Mission frames** define the purpose of the current run. They answer: what is this loop for right now? In Trident-G the core frames are Understand, Argue, Choose, Plan & Do, with Learn best treated as a user-facing acquisition frame rather than a separate architectural family.

**Control missions** protect viability. They answer: what keeps the loop clean and repeatable under distraction, pressure, fatigue, overheat, or flatness? Their targets include shielding attention, regulating cadence, prioritising missions, creating distance, calibrating mode, arbitrating improvisation versus script, recovering, and externalising when needed.

**Meta-dials** shape the long-timescale geometry of cognition. They answer: what slower governance variables alter controllability, learnability, corridor width, ecological support, proof discipline, compression depth, scale control, and the overall quality of loop execution across time? The current canonical set includes meta-efficacy, coherence, compression depth, complexity governance, subjective calibration, proof discipline, progress–quality governance, exploration–exploitation governance, multi-scale governance, and ecology support.

**Feedback signals** provide fast diagnostics. They answer: what kind of adjustment is needed now? These include load, drift, mismatch, unexpected mismatch, salience, complexity, and conflict.

This distinction is crucial. The loop is the kernel. The four layers tell it what to optimise, what to protect, what shapes its geometry over time, and what kind of update is currently required. But the loop itself remains the mechanism that turns those inputs into viable inference, selective compression, and reusable competence.

## X5. The central bridge: relational processing and reasoning

The live centre of the kernel is relational processing and reasoning. This is what allows the loop to operate on structure rather than on surface cues alone. It lets the system hold, compare, transform, and test relations: what connects to what, what constrains what, what predicts what, and what matters most under current stakes. A good map is therefore both a predictive structure and a value landscape.

Relational reasoning is the live bridge between corridor-controlled viability and the banked Gc library. It coordinates movement between exploration and exploitation while remaining anchored to what has already been banked. This is what makes the G-Loop an adaptive inference engine rather than a motivational slogan.

It is also what makes gist extraction possible. Insight in Trident-G is not merely a flash or a pleasing interpretation. It is the progressive extraction of deeper structure from a live relational field: what relation is actually being tracked, what invariant explains why something worked, what can be preserved across changing wrappers, and what should be discarded as surface detail. In operator terms, this compression depends especially on mapping, chunking, abstraction, re-representation, meta-mapping, evaluation, and portability checking.

In practical terms, this bridge depends on four capacities: relational fluid intelligence, working memory, attention management, and relational inference/updating capacity. Together they make the inference algebra runnable under load and make Gf discoveries bankable as Gc when they survive validation.

## X6. The G-Loop process

The whole G-Loop process can be described as the following recurring cycle.

### X6.1 Enter or restore the workable corridor

Every loop begins with viability. The first question is not “What is the smartest move?” but “Is the system in a workable state to run a clean loop?” The Ψ-band is the range of mental load in which the system can still think clearly enough to learn and make good moves. Above it lies overheat, thrash, and panic-grind. Below it lies flatness, boredom-drift, or protective disengagement.

If the system is out of band, the first move is not deeper inference but recovery, simplification, re-entry micro-steps, or externalisation.

At the lived level, this stage is closely related to current challenge state: the present runtime burden of engaging the task cleanly, including working-memory load, attention demand, control stability, drift risk, emotional pressure, and viable bandwidth for keeping a usable model online for understanding or action.

### X6.2 Set the mission frame

Once viable, the loop is run for a purpose. This purpose may be to understand, argue, choose, or plan and do. The frame determines what counts as a good map, a good test, a good update, a good gist, and a clean stop. The same kernel can run in every frame, but the success criterion changes by frame.

This is why the kernel is portable across domains. In a research system the current frame may be explanation or discrimination. In a trading system it may be choose or plan and do. In a business ops kernel it may be prioritise, decide, execute, and monitor.

### X6.3 Set rigour through salience

Salience determines how much loop to run. It integrates stakes, uncertainty, time, and state, and sets the rigour budget. Low salience supports a lighter loop with cheap probes and faster commitment. High salience or high mismatch justifies richer mapping, stronger checks, and more cautious stopping rules.

Salience is therefore not a side-signal. It is the budgeting dial for the whole kernel, including how much modelling, testing, and compression effort is warranted now.

At the lived level, salience and load contribute strongly to perceived effort: the organism’s estimate of the total cost of running the loop under current stakes and state, both now and in anticipated near-future demand.

In practice, the quality of rigour allocation is also shaped by the meta-dial layer, especially coherence, subjective calibration, proof discipline, progress–quality governance, exploration–exploitation governance, and multi-scale governance.

### X6.4 Build the map

The map is the current usable representation of the situation. It includes both predictive structure and value landscape: what tends to lead to what, what is constrained, what options exist, and what matters under the present stakes.

In stack terms, the map sits within the semantic layer: ontology provides the bounded world, the situation map provides the current usable model, and the affordance surface provides the currently available probes, moves, and actions.

The map must obey the accuracy–complexity rule. It should be the simplest map that is accurate enough to guide the next move. When load or drift is high, simplify hard. When mismatch is high, allow more structure briefly to find the missing constraint, wrong relation, or wrong value assumption.

This stage is also the beginning of compression. The map is not a full copy of reality. It is already a selective representation shaped by current purpose, stakes, and viability.

The term complexity in the main G-Loop signal board refers primarily to this map-cost sense. This may be called Trident complexity: how costly it is to hold and run the current map for understanding or action. It is the representational burden or model weight of the map: how many moving parts it has, how hard it is to hold online, and how fragile it becomes under load and drift.

Good mapping also depends on selecting the right scale: some situations call for broad strategic framing, others for local technical detail, and many require clean movement between these levels across time.

### X6.5 Infer through the live centre

Inference is not a single stage after mapping. It is the live central process that works through the map, transforms it, compares alternatives, extracts implications, and routes the system between widening and tightening. This live centre is Gf: flexible reasoning under novelty and uncertainty.

The 2×2 mode selector helps describe how this inferential centre moves:

- explore vs exploit
- meaning vs moves

Meaning plus explore widens the map. Meaning plus exploit pins the gist. Moves plus explore tries probes. Moves plus exploit executes and stabilises.

This is the stage at which insight becomes most visible. The system is not merely generating options. It is actively trying to discover what deeper relation, explanation, trade-off, route, or rule best compresses the current situation. In Trident-G terms, the gist is not a slogan. It is the best current compression of the structurally important part of the map, suitable for later testing, commitment, and possible banking.

At the lived level, this stage may be tracked partly through compression success or failure: how successfully the system can reduce current structure into a usable gist, invariant, or next move without losing what matters. Successful compression is often experienced as relief, coherence, click, confidence, or excitement. But reward from compression is not yet proof of transfer.

### X6.6 Test against reality

Testing is what keeps the kernel honest. It prevents plausible stories from being mistaken for portable competence. The loop uses two forms of test.

A **probe** is a cheap, discriminating check that reveals what is wrong or separates competing alternatives.  
A **validation** is a stronger check that asks whether the candidate solution generalises, survives variation, and has known boundaries and traps.

High mismatch usually calls for a probe first. Banking a script requires validation, typically including a syntax swap, wrapper swap, or stake swap while holding deep operator demand as constant as possible.

Testing therefore does more than correct error. It also determines whether the extracted gist reflects deep structure or only a wrapper-bound trick.

### X6.7 Commit and satisfice

The loop must stop hovering. Commitment means choosing a concrete next action, test, or plan step. Satisficing means stopping when there is enough signal to act or test, rather than continuing to model indefinitely. This protects the Ψ-band by preventing endless over-processing.

High-salience loops justify stronger validation before stopping. Low-salience loops can stop after a cheap probe and a clear commit.

Commitment also stabilises the current gist candidate. Without commitment, the system never has to make explicit what it currently takes the point, rule, trade-off, or next move to be. In that sense, commitment is the point at which an emerging insight becomes concrete enough to be acted on, challenged, and later compiled.

In training terms, this also relates to progress–quality governance: the higher-order ability to move through the problem space with enough rigour for the current stakes, without collapsing into either perfectionistic hovering or premature low-quality closure.

### X6.8 Act, observe, and update

In real use, especially in Plan & Do, commitment is followed by action and observation. The system then updates the map, probe design, gist, or commitment policy under feedback. This means the kernel is not purely contemplative. It is an active inference loop.

This update phase is where mismatch matters most. The purpose of mismatch is not to trigger more effort by default, but to tell the system that the current map, extracted gist, assumption set, or chosen move is off.

In interpretive terms, this is also where one may locate FEP complexity: how costly it is to revise the current model, beliefs, policy, or skill structure in order to fit what is happening. In Trident-G terms, this is felt mainly through mismatch and unexpected mismatch, which drive updating versus deeper restructuring. This is not a separate right-side signal on the Trident board. It is the inferential revision cost that sits behind mismatch-driven updating.

### X6.9 Install and bank what survives

Banking is the crystallisation phase. It is not just saving a memory of what worked. It is compiling a validated mindware script: a cue-fired, reusable routine with steps, checks, boundaries, traps, and a stop rule. Gc in Trident-G is validated memory, not just stored experience.

What gets banked is not the whole episode. It is the portable compression of what survived the episode. That may be a rule, invariant, operator chain, decision principle, map fragment, cue-pattern, or structured gist that preserves what matters while discarding wrapper-bound detail.

Install / Bank should therefore be treated as a cross-frame conversion mode that can follow any successful mission. A candidate becomes bankable only after it survives portability checks and is linked to real or strongly mission-proximal success signals, not merely local reward in one wrapper.

The practical banked unit remains:

`cue / trigger → steps or operators → check → boundaries or traps → stop rule`

But behind that unit sits a deeper principle: banking is the conversion of fluid insight into compact, portable, re-instantiable structure.

## X7. The signal board and routing logic

The G-Loop kernel is governed operationally by a compact signal board.

- **Load** tracks how much burden is actually being carried and whether the current loop remains viable.  
- **Drift** tracks task-set decay and warns when credit assignment is getting noisy.  
- **Mismatch** tracks the gap between what was braced for and what actually occurred.  
- **Unexpected mismatch** tracks surprise about mismatch itself and acts as the escalation signal for deeper restructuring.  
- **Salience** sets rigour.  
- **Complexity** tracks the weight of the current map.  
- **Conflict** flags local competition or incompatibility and usually routes towards sharper evaluation or discrimination.

These signals are not seven equal lights. Load, mismatch, and unexpected mismatch are the main state and error signals. Drift is a control-quality warning. Salience is the budget dial. Complexity is a map-cost dial. Conflict is a cue source.

The default routing rule is:

1. Check load and drift first. If either is too high, simplify, stabilise, recover, or externalise before drawing strong conclusions.
2. Then use mismatch to decide whether the current map or policy is off.
3. Then use unexpected mismatch to decide whether ordinary tuning is insufficient and structural revision is needed.
4. Then use salience to decide how much loop to run.
5. Then use complexity to prevent the map from collapsing under its own weight.
6. Then use conflict to design sharper local checks.

These signals therefore govern not only whether the system keeps going, but whether it should widen, compress, probe, or restructure the current candidate gist.

### X7.1 Clarifying complexity, effort, and challenge inside the loop

Several useful interpretive distinctions can now be stated explicitly.

**FEP complexity**  
How costly it is to revise the current model, beliefs, policy, or skill structure in order to fit what is happening. In Trident-G terms, this is felt mainly through mismatch and unexpected mismatch, which drive updating versus deeper restructuring.

**Trident complexity**  
How costly it is to hold and run the current map for understanding or action. This is the representational burden or model weight of the map: how many moving parts it has, how hard it is to hold online, and how fragile it becomes under load and drift.

**Perceived effort**  
The lived estimate of the total cost of running the loop under current stakes and state, both now and in anticipated near-future demand. This includes the felt cost of updating, holding the map, sustaining attention, managing conflict, and staying viable enough to continue. This is not a formal Trident-G scalar by itself, but it fits well with the framework’s treatment of load, salience, and subjectivity as the lived interface of significance and cost.

**Current challenge state**  
The present runtime burden of engaging the task cleanly: working-memory load, attention demand, control stability, drift risk, emotional pressure, and viable bandwidth for keeping a usable model online for understanding or action.

**Compression success or failure**  
How successfully the system can reduce current structure into a usable gist, invariant, or next move without losing what matters. This is implied by the G-Loop’s “meaning plus exploit pins the gist”, by abstraction as extracting deeper invariants, and by banking as validated portable competence. Successful compression often feels pleasurable or exciting, but that reward is not proof of far transfer.

**Enjoyable challenge**  
A state in which complexity is high enough to be engaging and arousing, but still low enough for the system to update, compress, and act successfully. In Trident-G language, this is close to an in-band state where salience is meaningful but the map remains viable and does not collapse under its own weight.

A concise relation among these is:

> Mismatch tells the loop that revision may be needed, complexity tells it how heavy the current map is, perceived effort is the lived estimate of total loop cost, and enjoyable challenge happens when the system can still update, compress, and act without losing viable control.

## X8. Reinforcement and learning inside the kernel

Reinforcement learning is central to the kernel, but it is not the whole story. The framework is explicit that local reward is insufficient for far transfer. A policy may be strengthened or weakened quickly through mismatch and outcome signals, but it should only be promoted into Gc when it later survives swaps, traps, and real missions.

This gives the kernel several distinct learning roles.

1. First, it tunes execution and re-entry under load.  
2. Second, it updates the current model or policy through mismatch.  
3. Third, when mismatch becomes unexpectedly unstable and routine re-entry fails, it escalates into deeper restructuring of schema, boundary, trigger, state abstraction, or representational level.  
4. Fourth, it selectively compresses what survives into a portable invariant, gist, or reusable script.

Compression success is often intrinsically rewarding, and that reward may support motivation and learning. But the loop must not mistake pleasurable compression for validated transfer. A satisfying click is a candidate signal of emerging gist, not proof that the resulting structure will travel.

So the G-Loop is not just a decision loop. It is also a selective crystallisation loop.

## X9. Why this kernel is domain-general

The kernel is domain-general because it is defined in terms of viability, mapping, probing, gist extraction, commitment, updating, and banking, rather than in terms of any one content area. The same core logic can therefore be applied to:

- biological cognition and brain-level intelligent action
- cognitive training and far-transfer protocol design
- research workflows and scientific reasoning
- decision and action systems in business
- human-plus-AI operational stacks that need inspectable inference, prioritisation, action, and learning over time

In that sense, the G-Loop is best understood as the intelligence kernel of Trident-G, while the inference algebra specifies the primitive moves by which that kernel executes itself.

The G-Loop is the core intelligence kernel of Trident-G: the smallest repeatable cycle that keeps cognition viable inside the defended Ψ corridor while turning successful inference into reusable competence. It runs under a mission frame, within a wider architecture shaped by meta-missions, meta-dials, and fast signals, allocates rigour through salience, builds the simplest map that is accurate enough for the next move, uses live relational reasoning to widen or tighten as needed, extracts the gist or deeper invariant of what matters, tests against reality by probe and validation, commits through satisficing rather than endless modelling, updates under load, drift, and mismatch, and banks only what survives portability checks into Gc as portable mindware. Because its logic is domain-general, the same kernel can organise biological cognition, training systems, research workflows, and human-plus-tool operational stacks.
