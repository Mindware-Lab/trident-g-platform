## Appendix X. The G-Loop as the core intelligence kernel of Trident-G

### X0. Purpose

The G-Loop is the core intelligence kernel of Trident-G. It is the smallest repeatable cycle that both keeps cognition viable inside the defended Ψ corridor and converts successful inference into reusable know-how. It is therefore not merely a training loop, a motivational cycle, or a problem-solving checklist. It is a general control-and-learning kernel for intelligent action under constraint.

In Trident-G, the same kernel can be run for different purposes and in different substrates. At the human level, it describes how intelligent cognition stays viable, updates its model, compresses and extracts what matters, and crystallises reusable competence. At the applied level, it can organise training, decision workflows, research systems, business operations, or other human-plus-tool environments, because the kernel itself is defined in domain-general terms: **build the map, choose a strategy, deploy tactics through operators, test, commit, update, and bank** under signals, constraints, and mission criteria.

A crucial clarification is that the loop is not only about producing a correct next move. It is also about constructing a workable problem space, selecting an appropriate strategy for that space, deploying the right local tactics, choosing the smallest useful discriminating test, extracting the gist of what is structurally important, and compiling what survives into reusable competence. In that sense, the G-Loop is both an adaptive inference loop and a selective compression loop.

### X1. Core definition

The G-Loop is the smallest repeatable cycle that:

keeps the system inside, or restores it to, a workable range of viable operation, the Ψ-band, and

turns good thinking plus real checks into reusable know-how.

In everyday terms, it is a **think–check–bank** loop. In theoretical terms, it is a viability-gated adaptive inference cycle that allocates rigour, builds the simplest usable problem-space map, selects a workable strategy, deploys tactics through relational reasoning and operator execution, tests by discriminating probe and stronger validation, commits to a workable next move, updates confidence and structure under feedback, and crystallises what survives into portable Gc.

The loop should therefore be understood as doing two things at once. It supports viable intelligent action in the present. It also compresses and compiles what survives into reusable structure for future use.

### X2. Why it is a kernel, not just a training loop

The G-Loop should be treated as the kernel because everything else in Trident-G depends on it.

Mission frames do not replace it. They specify what the loop is being run for. Control missions do not replace it. They keep it viable. Meta-dials do not replace it. They shape the longer-timescale geometry within which it runs. Feedback signals do not replace it. They diagnose what sort of update is needed now.

That is why the G-Loop can be treated as the common engine across very different applications. The purpose changes. The kernel does not. What changes is the mission frame, the success signal, the data source, the wrapper, and the action surface. What stays constant is the control-and-inference logic.

This includes not only mapping, probing, commitment, and updating, but also problem-space navigation, strategy selection, tactic deployment, selective compression, and the separation of local success from structurally portable competence.

### X3. Highest-level architecture

At the deepest level, Trident-G is organised as a shaft-and-tines structure.

The shaft is Gc: the banked library of validated scripts, chunks, concepts, compressed invariants, and crystallised competence.

The centre tine is Gf: live, effortful, flexible reasoning.

The outer tines are the two dominant higher-engagement branches: entropy-widening exploration and mutual-information-tightening control.

The G-Loop is the operational cycle that coordinates these parts. It keeps live reasoning anchored to viable state, allows widening or tightening as needed, builds and searches a workable problem space, selects strategies, deploys tactics, runs operators, extracts the gist of what the current structure implies, and crystallises what survives into reusable competence.

Above this sit two highest-level meta-missions:

**Viability**: maintain adaptive operation within the defended Ψ corridor so learning, inference, and action remain clean and recoverable.

**Expansion**: enlarge viable dynamic range, portable operator repertoire, tactical flexibility, and strategic range through the coordination of exploration, exploitation, gist extraction, validation, and crystallisation into Gc.

These two meta-missions explain what the kernel is for in the broadest sense. In training terms, they should also be treated as trainable global orientations: a cultivated preference for stable, recoverable in-band function and for enlarging live reasoning range and banked portable competence, rather than merely securing local wrapper-bound success.

### X4. The four surrounding layers

To describe the G-Loop cleanly, four surrounding layers should be kept distinct.

**Mission frames** define the purpose of the current run. They answer: what is this loop for right now?

**Control missions** protect viability. They answer: what keeps the loop clean and repeatable under distraction, pressure, fatigue, overheat, or flatness?

**Meta-dials** shape the long-timescale geometry of cognition. They answer: what slower governance variables alter controllability, proof discipline, compression depth, exploration–exploitation balance, scale control, and overall loop quality across time?

**Feedback signals** provide fast diagnostics. They answer: what kind of adjustment is needed now? These include load, drift, mismatch, unexpected mismatch, salience, complexity, and conflict.

This distinction is crucial. The loop is the kernel. The surrounding layers tell it what to optimise, what to protect, what shapes its geometry over time, and what kind of update is currently required. But the loop itself remains the mechanism that turns those inputs into viable inference, selective compression, and reusable competence.

### X5. The live centre: relational processing, operators, tactics, and strategies

The live centre of the kernel is relational processing and reasoning. This is what allows the loop to operate on structure rather than on surface cues alone. It lets the system hold, compare, transform, and test relations: what connects to what, what constrains what, what predicts what, what route remains open, what blocks what, and what matters most under current stakes.

A good map is therefore not only predictive structure and value landscape. It is also a **workable problem space**: a representation of states, constraints, options, paths, bottlenecks, boundaries, reachable goals, candidate tactics, and candidate next moves.

Relational reasoning is the live bridge between corridor-controlled viability and the banked Gc library. It coordinates movement between exploration and exploitation while remaining anchored to what has already been banked. This is what makes the G-Loop an adaptive inference engine rather than a motivational slogan.

It is also what makes gist extraction possible. Insight in Trident-G is not merely a flash or a pleasing interpretation. It is the progressive extraction of deeper structure from a live relational field: what relation is actually being tracked, what invariant explains why something worked, what route or constraint really matters, what strategy is appropriate, what tactic should be applied locally, what can be preserved across changing wrappers, and what should be discarded as surface detail.

#### X5.1 Operators, tactics, and strategies inside the live centre

To keep the architecture clear, three internal levels should be distinguished.

**Operators** are the primitive Y-moves of the system. They are the reusable rule-like mindware moves that act on maps, chunks, schemas, claims, hypotheses, action candidates, and external artefacts. Operators are the smallest portable units of intelligent action in the formal move-language.

**Tactics** are local, reusable compositions of operators. A tactic is a structured way of making progress at a particular point in a problem space, such as narrowing constraints, perturbing the space, stabilising a working hypothesis, or guarding connectivity. Tactics are more situation-shaped than single operators, but more local and specific than full strategies.

**Strategies** are broader ways of approaching a class of problems or a phase of a problem space. A strategy selects and sequences tactics in service of the current mission frame. Strategies therefore sit above tactics and below mission frames: they are not the ultimate purpose of the run, but the broader approach by which the system tries to succeed in that run.

This keeps the architecture clean. The operator set remains compact. Tactics do not become extra primitives. Strategies do not replace mission frames. Instead, the live centre can now be described more clearly as a place where **strategies organise the search, tactics solve local separations, and operators execute the actual moves**.  

#### X5.2 Strategy families inside the live centre

The updated problem-solving material makes it useful to name six applied strategy families that can operate inside the loop. These are not extra Y-operators. They are broader problem-space approaches that select tactics and organise operator use.

**Deductive strategies**
Used when the task requires necessary consequence, exclusion, contradiction handling, or local certainty. This includes modus ponens, modus tollens, constraint propagation, and counterexample or contradiction testing.

**Exploratory model-space strategies**
Used when the task requires generating or comparing rival explanations, reframings, or structural possibilities. This includes abduction, counterfactual simulation, analogical reasoning, and causal structure search.

**Probe-selection strategies**
Used when progress depends on choosing the most useful next question or test. This includes value-of-information, expected information gain, and discriminating test design.

**Search / route strategies**
Used when the system must move through a problem space or close the gap between current and goal state. This includes means–ends reasoning, bidirectional search, and operator sequencing.

**Update strategies**
Used when beliefs, confidence, or policy need to shift under evidence. This includes Bayesian or statistical updating and confidence-calibrated updating.

**Structural-map strategies**
Used when the deeper issue is network form, bottlenecks, paths, reachability, or the preservation of global structure. This includes graph-theoretic reasoning and connectivity or reachability reasoning.

These strategy families enrich the middle of the loop but do not change the architecture. The operator set remains compact. The tactics remain local compositions. The kernel stays the same. What changes is that the live centre is now described more explicitly as **strategy-guided problem-space search and tactic-level restructuring** rather than generic inference alone. 

#### X5.3 Canonical tactics inside the live centre

The updated problem-solving patterns are best treated as canonical tactics rather than new primitives.

**Constraint-induced narrowing**
Mainly Y12 + Y13. Use evaluation and probing to eliminate impossibilities and tighten the current space.

**Strategic perturbation**
Mainly Y13. Use a small, informative test to reveal hidden structure or separate rival explanations.

**Bidirectional constraint squeezing**
Mainly Y4 + Y11 + Y16 + Y12. Build the space from both ends, hold the goal state explicitly, and shrink the route space until a workable bridge appears.

**Working-hypothesis stabilisation**
Mainly Y15 + Y12. Commit defeasibly to the best current hypothesis so that it can be tested, updated, or abandoned cleanly.

**Forced-move commitment**
Mainly Y15. When the space is sufficiently constrained, commit to the only viable next move rather than hovering.

**Global connectivity guarding**
Mainly Y4 + Y11 + Y12. Preserve the viability of the whole structure by checking whether crucial nodes, paths, or bottlenecks remain intact.

**Confidence-calibrated updating**
Mainly Y12 + Y13. Update belief or policy in proportion to evidence quality and reliability rather than by mere emotional force or local fluency.

These tactics are not the kernel themselves. They are reusable forms of how the kernel thinks when operating over a structured problem space. 

### X6. The G-Loop process

The whole G-Loop process can be described as the following recurring cycle.

#### X6.1 Enter or restore the workable corridor

Every loop begins with viability. The first question is not “What is the smartest move?” but “Is the system in a workable state to run a clean loop?”

If the system is out of band, the first move is not deeper inference but recovery, simplification, re-entry micro-steps, or externalisation.

At the lived level, this stage is closely related to current challenge state: the present runtime burden of engaging the task cleanly, including working-memory load, attention demand, control stability, drift risk, emotional pressure, and viable bandwidth for keeping a usable model online for understanding or action.

#### X6.2 Set the mission frame

Once viable, the loop is run for a purpose. This purpose may be to understand, argue, choose, or plan and do. The frame determines what counts as a good map, a good strategy, a good tactic, a good test, a good update, a good gist, and a clean stop.

This is why the kernel is portable across domains. The frame changes. The kernel does not.

#### X6.3 Set rigour through salience

Salience determines how much loop to run. It integrates stakes, uncertainty, time, and state, and sets the rigour budget. Low salience supports a lighter loop with cheaper probes and faster commitment. High salience or high mismatch justifies richer mapping, stronger checks, and more cautious stopping rules.

Salience is therefore not a side-signal. It is the budgeting dial for the whole kernel.

#### X6.4 Build the problem-space map

The map is the current usable representation of the situation. It includes predictive structure and value landscape, but should now be understood more explicitly as a **problem-space map**.

This means the map may include:

current state and target state

entities, roles, and constraints

available operators or moves

candidate tactics

available paths, bottlenecks, and dependencies

reachable and unreachable states

local traps, boundaries, and near-misses

what matters under the present stakes

The map must obey the accuracy–complexity rule. It should be the simplest map that is accurate enough to guide the next move. When load or drift is high, simplify hard. When mismatch is high, allow more structure briefly to find the missing constraint, broken route, wrong relation, wrong value assumption, or wrong strategic framing.

#### X6.5 Select strategy, deploy tactics, and run operators through the live centre

Inference is not a single stage after mapping. It is the live central process that works through the map, compares alternatives, chooses a strategy, deploys tactics, runs operators, searches for routes, tests constraints, and routes the system between widening and tightening.

The 2×2 mode selector still helps describe how this inferential centre moves:

**Meaning + explore** widens the model space through abductive, analogical, counterfactual, or causal-search strategies.

**Meaning + exploit** pins the gist through abstraction, narrowing, and structural compression.

**Moves + explore** tries probes, perturbations, and informative tactical tests.

**Moves + exploit** executes means–ends reduction, route tightening, operator sequencing, and forced-move commitment.

This is also the stage at which structural-map strategies become important. Some problems are not mainly about choosing a claim or option, but about preserving connectivity, finding a path, locating a bottleneck, or proving that one route is blocked.

At the lived level, this stage may be tracked partly through compression success or failure: how successfully the system can reduce current structure into a usable gist, invariant, route, tactic, or next move without losing what matters.

#### X6.6 Test by discriminating probe, then validate

Testing is what keeps the kernel honest. It prevents plausible stories from being mistaken for portable competence.

A **probe** is a cheap, discriminating check that reveals what is wrong or separates competing alternatives.

A **validation** is a stronger check that asks whether the candidate solution generalises, survives variation, and has known boundaries and traps.

The updated tactic language sharpens this point. Test is not merely “check whether right”. It often means:

designing a discriminating probe

searching for a counterexample

forcing a contradiction test

running a smallest useful perturbation

checking whether a route is still reachable

asking which observation would actually separate hypothesis A from hypothesis B

High mismatch usually calls for a probe first. Banking a script requires stronger validation, typically including syntax swap, wrapper swap, stake swap, and boundary or trap checks. 

#### X6.7 Stabilise the working hypothesis and commit

The loop must stop hovering. Commitment means choosing a concrete next action, test, or plan step. Satisficing means stopping when there is enough signal to act or test, rather than continuing to model indefinitely.

This stage is now best described not just as generic commitment, but often as **working-hypothesis stabilisation**. The system commits defeasibly to the best current route, explanation, tactic, or strategy strongly enough that it can be tested, updated, or replaced cleanly.

Sometimes the right move is full local commitment because constraints have exhausted the alternatives. Sometimes it is provisional commitment with explicit expectation of revision. In both cases, commitment stabilises the current gist candidate and makes real updating possible.

#### X6.8 Act, observe, and update

In real use, especially in Plan & Do, commitment is followed by action and observation. The system then updates the map, tactic choice, strategic framing, gist, or commitment policy under feedback.

This update phase is where mismatch matters most. The purpose of mismatch is not to trigger more effort by default, but to tell the system that the current map, route, extracted gist, tactic, strategy, assumption set, or chosen move is off.

The updated taxonomy strengthens this phase by making explicit the need for **confidence-calibrated updating**. Not all evidence deserves the same weight. Good updating revises belief or policy in proportion to evidence quality, reliability, and diagnostic value. Weak signals justify small updates. Strong signals justify larger ones.

When mismatch is acute, simplification and tightening often come first. When mismatch is persistent or unexpectedly unstable, deeper restructuring becomes more likely. That is the point at which the system may need to revise the representation, route, boundary, trigger, tactic, strategy, or state abstraction itself.

#### X6.9 Install and bank what survives

Banking is the crystallisation phase. It is not just saving a memory of what worked. It is compiling a validated mindware script: a cue-fired, reusable routine with steps, checks, boundaries, traps, and a stop rule.

What gets banked is not the whole episode. It is the portable compression of what survived the episode. That may be a rule, invariant, operator chain, tactic, strategic principle, route fragment, map fragment, cue-pattern, or structured gist that preserves what matters while discarding wrapper-bound detail.

Install / Bank should therefore be treated as a cross-frame conversion mode that can follow any successful mission. A candidate becomes bankable only after it survives portability checks and is linked to real or strongly mission-proximal success signals, not merely local reward in one wrapper.

The practical banked unit remains:

**cue / trigger → steps, operators, or tactic → check → boundaries or traps → stop rule**

### X7. The signal board and routing logic

The G-Loop kernel is governed operationally by a compact signal board.

**Load** tracks how much burden is actually being carried and whether the current loop remains viable.

**Drift** tracks task-set decay and warns when credit assignment is getting noisy.

**Mismatch** tracks the gap between what was braced for and what actually occurred.

**Unexpected mismatch** tracks surprise about mismatch itself and acts as the escalation signal for deeper restructuring.

**Salience** sets rigour.

**Complexity** tracks the weight of the current map.

**Conflict** flags local competition or incompatibility and usually routes towards sharper evaluation or discrimination.

These signals are not seven equal lights. Load, mismatch, and unexpected mismatch are the main state and error signals. Drift is a control-quality warning. Salience is the budget dial. Complexity is a map-cost dial. Conflict is a cue source.

The default routing rule is:

check load and drift first

then use mismatch to decide whether the current map, tactic, or strategy is off

then use unexpected mismatch to decide whether ordinary tuning is insufficient and structural revision is needed

then use salience to decide how much loop to run

then use complexity to prevent the map from collapsing under its own weight

then use conflict to design a sharper local check

The updated problem-solving material sharpens the final step. Conflict is often best treated as a sign that a **local separation problem** exists and that the loop should run a discriminating tactic such as a contradiction test, counterexample search, perturbation, or connectivity check rather than more generic effort. 

### X8. Reinforcement and learning inside the kernel

Reinforcement learning is central to the kernel, but it is not the whole story. The framework is explicit that local reward is insufficient for far transfer. A policy, tactic, or strategy may be strengthened or weakened quickly through mismatch and outcome signals, but it should only be promoted into Gc when it later survives swaps, traps, and real missions.

This gives the kernel several distinct learning roles.

First, it tunes execution and re-entry under load.

Second, it updates the current map, tactic, or strategy through mismatch.

Third, when mismatch becomes unexpectedly unstable and routine re-entry fails, it escalates into deeper restructuring of schema, route, boundary, trigger, tactic, strategy, state abstraction, or representational level.

Fourth, it selectively compresses what survives into a portable invariant, gist, tactic, or reusable script.

Compression success is often intrinsically rewarding, and that reward may support motivation and learning. But the loop must not mistake pleasurable compression for validated transfer. A satisfying click is a candidate signal of emerging gist, not proof that the resulting structure will travel. 

### X9. Why this kernel is domain-general

The kernel is domain-general because it is defined in terms of viability, problem-space mapping, strategy selection, tactic deployment, discriminating probes, commitment, updating, and banking, rather than in terms of any one content area.

The same core logic can therefore be applied to:

biological cognition and brain-level intelligent action

cognitive training and far-transfer protocol design

research workflows and scientific reasoning

decision and action systems in business

human-plus-AI operational stacks that need inspectable inference, prioritisation, action, and learning over time

In that sense, the G-Loop is best understood as the intelligence kernel of Trident-G, while the operator algebra specifies the primitive moves by which that kernel executes itself, tactics specify reusable local composites of those moves, and strategies specify the broader problem-space approaches by which those tactics are selected and sequenced.  

### X10. Canonical one-paragraph definition

The G-Loop is the core intelligence kernel of Trident-G: the smallest repeatable cycle that keeps cognition viable inside the defended Ψ corridor while turning successful inference into reusable competence. It runs under a mission frame, within a wider architecture shaped by meta-missions, meta-dials, and fast signals, allocates rigour through salience, builds the simplest usable problem-space map, selects an appropriate strategy for that space, deploys tactics through live relational reasoning and operator execution, tests against reality by discriminating probe and stronger validation, stabilises and commits to a workable next move through satisficing rather than endless modelling, updates confidence and structure under load, drift, and mismatch, and banks only what survives portability checks into Gc as portable mindware. Because its logic is domain-general, the same kernel can organise biological cognition, training systems, research workflows, and human-plus-tool operational stacks.

That gives you a very clean stack.
