## Appendix X. High-level architecture of Trident-G: mission frames, language of thought and action, semantics, subjectivity, Ψ-allostatic geometry, and runtime

### X0. Purpose

This appendix sets out the high-level architecture of Trident-G in its most general form. Its aim is to clarify how the framework distinguishes:

- what the loop is **for**
- what the loop is **about**
- how the loop **thinks and acts**
- how the loop is shaped by **subjective valuation**
- under what **viability geometry** the loop remains clean and recoverable
- what **runtime substrate** makes the whole system executable

The result is a layered but integrated view of Trident-G as a domain-general intelligence kernel. The same G-Loop can be run across different mission frames, domains, data sources, and action surfaces, while preserving a common control-and-inference logic.

### X1. Mission frames come first

At the highest applied level, Trident-G begins with **mission frames**. These answer the question:

**What is the loop being run for right now?**

Mission frames define the purpose of the current run. They determine what counts as a good map, a good test, a good update, and a good stopping point for the current run. The same underlying algebra and operator set may be used in every frame, but the success criterion changes by frame.

The core mission frames are:

#### X1.1 Understand | Comprehension

Purpose: build a usable model of what is going on.  
Primary success criterion: a clearer map that predicts, explains, and supports a safe, informative next move.

#### X1.2 Argue | Argumentation

Purpose: produce, test, refine, or audit a claim.  
Primary success criterion: explicit claim-support discipline with validity checks, counter-consideration, and boundaries.

#### X1.3 Choose | Decision

Purpose: select an action under uncertainty and trade-offs.  
Primary success criterion: a justified choice with explicit trade-offs, acknowledged uncertainty, and a clear reason for selection.

#### X1.4 Plan & Do | Strategic action

Purpose: turn a map or choice into executable action, then adapt under feedback.  
Primary success criterion: an executable sequence with a measurable signal, monitoring, and real-world follow-through.

#### X1.5 Learn | Acquisition frame

Purpose: acquire, stabilise, and transfer a new capability, method, or schema.  
Primary success criterion: the learner can instantiate the skill or schema in a new wrapper, explain the cue, and show early portability. Architecturally, Learn is best treated as a user-facing acquisition frame, while Install / Bank remains a cross-frame conversion mode that can follow any successful mission.

Mission frames therefore define the **top-level purpose** of the run. They do not replace the kernel. They tell the kernel what counts as success here.

### X2. The wider Trident-G distinction already present in the framework

Mission frames sit inside a wider architecture already present in Trident-G. Four surrounding distinctions remain essential:

#### X2.1 Control missions

Control missions keep the loop viable, clean, and repeatable under distraction, pressure, fatigue, overheat, flatness, and fluctuating salience. Their target is not truth or performance directly, but clean loop execution inside the defended Ψ-band.

#### X2.2 Meta-dials

Meta-dials are slower shapers of cognitive geometry. Core examples include meta-efficacy, coherence, workspace synergies, and life-niche fit. They alter controllability, learnability, corridor width, ecological support, and noise over longer timescales.

#### X2.3 Feedback signals

Feedback signals are fast diagnostics. These include load, drift, mismatch, unexpected mismatch, salience, complexity, and conflict. They do not define the goal. They tell the loop whether to simplify, probe, recover, update, or restructure.

#### X2.4 G-Loop kernel

The G-Loop itself remains the core intelligence kernel: the smallest repeatable cycle that keeps cognition viable inside the defended Ψ corridor while turning successful inference into reusable competence. It runs by mapping, testing, committing, updating, and banking under mission criteria and signals.

The present appendix preserves these distinctions and adds further clarity to the formal, semantic, subjective, allostatic, and runtime sides of the system.

### X3. Language of thought and action

Trident-G can be described as having a higher-level **language of thought and action**.

**Language of thought and action  
= operator algebra + mission grammar**

This language is the compositional system by which intelligent moves are built and run.

#### X3.1 Operator algebra

The operator algebra is the set of primitive mindware moves by which the G-Loop sets rigour, builds and transforms structure, tests and discriminates, commits action, protects viability, and validates portability. These operators are not the raw processing substrate itself. They are rule-like moves that act on representational objects such as maps, chunks, schemas, claims, hypotheses, action candidates, and external artefacts.

In Trident-G, the canonical operator set is the Y1-Y16 family. These are compositional mindware moves that support both performance missions and resilience missions. The operator set belongs to the Mindware Layer rather than to the raw capacity substrate.

#### X3.2 Mission grammar

Mission grammar is the set of frame-specific well-formedness rules that governs how operators should be sequenced within a given mission frame. It specifies which sequences of moves are appropriate, diagnostically useful, and success-relevant in a given context.

The same operator algebra can therefore be used across frames, but the grammar differs because what counts as a good map, a good test, a good update, and a good stop differs between understanding, argumentation, choice, and execution.

So the operator algebra supplies the **vocabulary of intelligent moves**, while the mission grammar supplies the **compositional rules** for assembling them into a good run.

### X4. Reasoning calculus

Separate from the language of thought and action, though operating within it, is the **reasoning calculus**.

**Reasoning calculus  
= inferential transformations run within that language**

The existing Trident-G framework already distinguishes the operators from the cross-cutting reasoning styles used during operator execution. Deductive, inductive, abductive, analogical, Bayesian or statistical, and value-of-information reasoning are not separate top-level operators. They are inference styles layered onto operator runs. The operator says **what move is being made**, while the reasoning style says **how inference is being carried out during that move**.

This yields a clean formal architecture:

- **operator algebra** = what moves exist
- **mission grammar** = what sequences are well-formed for this purpose
- **reasoning calculus** = what inferential transformations are executed within those moves and sequences

### X5. Semantic layer

The formal language does not operate over empty symbols. Trident-G therefore benefits from an explicit **semantic layer**.

**Semantic layer**  
- ontology  
- situation map  
- affordance surface

This layer answers a different question from the formal layer. The formal layer says how the system can reason. The semantic layer says what the reasoning is *about*.

#### X5.1 Ontology

The ontology is the structured inventory of what exists in the relevant bounded world. It specifies the deep semantic structure of the domain: entities, states, relations, constraints, mechanisms, signals, values, risks, and affordances.

#### X5.2 Situation map

The situation map is the current usable representation of the present case. In Trident-G terms, a good map is both a **predictive structure** and a **value landscape**: what tends to lead to what, what is constrained, what options exist, and what matters under the present stakes.

#### X5.3 Affordance surface

The affordance surface is the live action layer exposed by the current map: what can be probed, changed, selected, delegated, monitored, deferred, or executed now.

Taken together, ontology, situation map, and affordance surface make explicit the modelled world over which the G-Loop runs. This fits the Trident-G claim that the kernel is domain-general and that what changes across applications is the mission frame, the success signal, the data source, and the action surface.

### X6. Subjectivity as the lived interface of the loop

Trident-G should treat **subjectivity** as architecturally central rather than as a mere by-product of reasoning. Feelings, motivations, emotions, urges, and felt significance are the lived interface through which the system encounters both the affordance surface and the underlying situation map.

This subjective interface registers not only what is present in the environment or model, but what is good or bad for the organism, what is costly or urgent, what is attractive or threatening, and what level of effort or caution is warranted now.

#### X6.1 Subjectivity as the phenomenal face of valuation

In Trident-G, a good map is not only predictive structure but also a **value landscape**. Subjectivity is how that value landscape is lived in conscious and instinctive control: pressure, confidence, dread, curiosity, relief, attraction, aversion, coherence, or confusion. These experiences do not replace formal inference, but they shape salience, attention allocation, action competition, and stop rules.

#### X6.2 Subjectivity as the interface to affordances

The affordance surface is not encountered as a flat menu of options. It is encountered through felt pull and push: approach, avoidance, urgency, dread, confidence, curiosity, frustration, or desire. In that sense, subjectivity is one of the ways the organism registers what matters, what is possible, and what should be acted on now.

#### X6.3 Subjectivity as signal-rich but fallible

Subjectivity should not be treated as either pure truth or pure noise. It is **signal-rich but fallible**. Some feelings disclose real significance. Others reflect over-bracing, threat bias, fatigue, flatness, stale priors, or distorted appraisal. For that reason, subjectivity should intersect with the feedback board rather than replace it. In practice, some feelings should be trusted as cues. Others should route the loop towards probing, distancing, simplification, recovery, or re-mapping.

#### X6.4 Subjectivity in training and coaching

In brain training and coaching, affective pressure should not be treated as irrelevant noise. Emotion can raise load, alter salience, bias action competition, destabilise the task-set, or distort the current map. Conversely, trained distancing, reappraisal, pacing, and recovery can restore viable inference and cleaner credit assignment. For this reason, state-gating and self-regulation are not peripheral to far transfer. They are part of the conditions under which portable operators can be learned without being confounded by hot-state over-bracing, flatness, panic-grind, or avoidance.

### X7. Ψ-allostatic geometry

Trident-G is not only a symbolic or inferential architecture. It is also a **state-sensitive intelligence architecture**. For that reason, the framework benefits from an explicit **Ψ-allostatic layer**.

**Allostatic layer**  
- Ψ-geometry / viability regime

This is the defended state-space geometry within which inference, learning, and action remain clean and recoverable. The framework already states that the G-Loop keeps cognition viable inside the defended Ψ corridor, and that the first question in any loop is whether the system is in a workable state to run a clean loop. Above the corridor lie overheat, thrash, and panic-grind. Below it lie flatness, boredom-drift, or protective disengagement.

Within this allostatic geometry:

- **load** tracks current realised burden
- **drift** tracks task-set decay
- **mismatch** tracks plan-reality or model-reality gap
- **unexpected mismatch** gates deeper restructuring
- **salience** sets rigour budget
- **complexity** tracks map cost
- **conflict** acts as a cue for sharper evaluation or discrimination

The allostatic layer is therefore not another mission family. It is the **viability geometry** within which the other layers operate.

#### X7.1 Subjectivity and allostasis

Subjectivity should be treated as the lived surface of allostatic control. Contemporary neuroscience links emotional experience closely to interoception and to allostatic regulation, supporting the idea that feelings are part of how the organism tracks bodily significance and prepares action rather than something external to intelligent control.

A cautious Trident-G interpretation is that subjectivity is rooted in **interoceptive-allostatic processing within a defended near-critical viability regime**. The allostasis part is well supported in current neuroscience; the near-critical or Griffiths-like framing is better treated as a systems-level interpretation of why the brain may benefit from an extended viable regime with both flexibility and stability.

### X8. Cognitive runtime

Below the formal, semantic, subjective, and allostatic layers sits the **cognitive runtime**.

#### X8.1 Runtime, broadly

The runtime is the whole executable substrate:

- working memory
- attention
- control stability
- interference resistance
- re-entry quality
- bandwidth, or viable computation under load

The Trident-G files already make this distinction. Working memory, attention protection, interference control, re-entry quality, and relational holding power belong primarily to the Capacity Layer. The operators themselves are not the raw processing. They are the rule-like moves that act on what the runtime keeps active and manipulable.

The G-Loop also states that the live centre depends on four practical capacities: relational fluid intelligence, working memory, attention management, and relational inference or updating capacity. Together these make the inference algebra runnable under load and make Gf discoveries bankable as Gc when they survive validation.

#### X8.2 Fast relational runtime: SR-like predictive bridge

Within the broader runtime, there can also be a **fast predictive relational layer**.

This narrower layer can be understood as an **SR-like predictive relational bridge** that:

- carries local structure forward
- supports multi-step expectation
- tracks likely next states and relations
- provides a live map that operators can act over

This fits the Trident-G claim that relational processing and reasoning is the live bridge between corridor-controlled viability and the banked Gc library, and that it allows the loop to hold, compare, transform, and test relations rather than merely react to surface cues.

It is best treated as a computational middle layer within the runtime rather than as the whole runtime itself.

### X9. Neurobiological interpretation, cautiously stated

A plausible neurobiological reading of this architecture is that hippocampal systems act as a relational binding and integration hub whose coupling profile shifts with task demands. During flexible inferential reasoning, hippocampal interactions with prefrontal and broader frontoparietal control systems may support recombination, comparison, and testing of relational content. During schema-rich modelling and deeper conceptual integration, hippocampal and parahippocampal interactions with default-mode cortical systems may support retrieval, integration, and elaboration of broader situation models and knowledge structures. This should be treated as a neurobiological interpretation consistent with Trident-G, not as a settled canonical claim of the framework.

### X10. Integrated stack

Putting the pieces together, the high-level Trident-G stack can be described as follows:

```text
Mission frames
  -> Understand | Argue | Choose | Plan & Do | Learn

Language of thought and action
  = operator algebra + mission grammar

Reasoning calculus
  = inferential transformations run within that language

Semantic layer
  -> ontology
  -> situation map
  -> affordance surface

Subjective interface
  -> feelings
  -> motivations
  -> emotions
  -> felt salience / significance
  -> urges, aversions, confidence, pressure

Allostatic layer
  -> Ψ-geometry / viability regime

Cognitive runtime
  -> working memory
  -> attention
  -> control stability
  -> interference resistance
  -> re-entry quality
  -> viable computation under load

Fast relational runtime
  -> SR-like predictive relational bridge
```

This stack should not be read as a set of isolated modules. The layers are nested and mutually dependent.

- Mission frames specify what success means.
- The language of thought and action specifies what structured move sequences are available.
- The reasoning calculus specifies how inference transforms maps, claims, options, and commitments within those sequences.
- The semantic layer specifies what the system is modelling.
- The subjective interface specifies how significance, cost, possibility, and threat are lived by the organism.
- The allostatic layer specifies whether the system is in a viable regime to reason cleanly.
- The runtime specifies what can actually be held, transformed, and executed.

### X11. Relation to the G-Loop kernel

The G-Loop remains the core kernel running across all these layers. It is still the smallest repeatable cycle that keeps cognition viable inside the defended Ψ corridor while converting successful inference into reusable competence. It still runs by mapping, testing, committing, updating, and banking under mission criteria and signals.

What this appendix adds is a clearer statement of what each layer contributes:

- **mission frames** tell the loop what it is for
- **the language of thought and action** tells the loop what formal moves and sequences it can use
- **the reasoning calculus** tells the loop how inferential transformation proceeds within those moves
- **the semantic layer** tells the loop what bounded world it is modelling
- **the subjective interface** tells the loop how significance is lived and valued
- **the allostatic layer** tells the loop whether the current operating regime is viable
- **the runtime** tells the loop what can actually be executed under current conditions

### X12. Canonical summary

Trident-G should be understood as a domain-general intelligence kernel organised across several interacting layers. Mission frames define the purpose of the current run. A language of thought and action, composed of an operator algebra and a mission grammar, defines the reusable mindware moves and their well-formed sequencing. A reasoning calculus specifies the principal inferential transformations carried out within that language. A semantic layer, consisting of ontology, situation map, and affordance surface, defines the bounded world the system is modelling. A subjective interface, consisting of feelings, motivations, emotions, and felt significance, is the lived valuation surface through which the organism encounters that world. A Ψ-allostatic layer defines the defended viability geometry within which inference, learning, and action remain clean and recoverable. A cognitive runtime, including working memory, attention, stability, interference resistance, re-entry, and viable bandwidth under load, makes the whole system executable, while a fast SR-like relational bridge provides a live predictive middle layer for structure-sensitive reasoning. Together these layers explain how Trident-G integrates purpose, modelling, subjectivity, inference, action, state control, and runtime execution into a single architecture.
